### TL;DR
Wikipedia entry on Eugene Wigner’s 1959 lecture arguing that mathematics works “unreasonably” well in physics, with the article outlining Wigner’s examples, later responses by Hamming/Tegmark/others, and earlier philosophical antecedents.

### Key Quote
“the laws of nature are written in the language of mathematics”

### Summary
- **What this page is about**
  - A reference article on **Eugene Wigner’s** lecture **“The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics in the Natural Sciences”**.
  - The lecture was delivered as the **1959 Richard Courant Lecture in Mathematical Sciences** at **New York University** and published in **Communications in Pure and Applied Mathematics** in **1960**.
  - Core claim: **pure mathematics, developed independently of physics, often ends up describing the physical world with surprising accuracy**.

- **Wigner’s main argument**
  - Mathematics often begins as an abstract pursuit driven by internal elegance and creativity, not by direct physical application.
  - Yet those abstractions later become central in physics and can even guide theory development and prediction.
  - Wigner presents this as a longstanding mystery about why the natural world is so well captured by mathematical language.

- **Examples Wigner uses**
  - **Newtonian gravitation**
    - Originally formulated for falling bodies, then extended from “very scanty observations” to planetary motion.
    - Wigner emphasizes the unexpected accuracy of this extension.
  - **Quantum mechanics / matrix mechanics**
    - Max Born recognized formal similarity between Heisenberg’s rules and matrix calculus.
    - Born, Jordan, and Heisenberg replaced position and momentum variables with matrices.
    - The approach was later confirmed by accurate results for the **hydrogen atom**, then refined calculations for **helium** reportedly matched experiment to **one part in ten million**.
  - **Quantum electrodynamics / Lamb shift**
    - Wigner treats the Lamb shift calculation by **Bethe** and **Schwinger** as an especially striking case of a largely mathematical theory with excellent experimental agreement.
    - He says the agreement was **better than one part in a thousand**.

- **Other examples added by the article**
  - **Maxwell’s equations**: developed for electricity and magnetism but later shown to describe **radio waves**.
  - **General relativity**: depended on the development of **tensor analysis** by mathematicians such as **Gauss, Riemann, Christoffel, Ricci-Curbastro, and Levi-Civita**.

- **Responses and follow-on work**
  - The article lists later works inspired by or reacting to Wigner:
    - **Richard Hamming** – “The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics”
    - **Arthur Lesk** – in molecular biology
    - **Peter Norvig** – “The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Data”
    - **Max Tegmark** – “The Mathematical Universe”
    - **Ivor Grattan-Guinness** – argued the effectiveness is reasonable and partly due to analogy/generalization/metaphor
    - **Vela Velupillai** – “The Unreasonable Ineffectiveness of Mathematics in Economics”
    - **Terrence Joseph Sejnowski** – on deep learning in AI

- **Richard Hamming’s partial explanations**
  - Hamming proposes four explanations, but says none fully solves the mystery:
    1. **Humans notice what they look for** and may shape physics through mathematical thinking.
    2. **Humans create/select mathematics that fits** a situation; new tools like vectors and tensors emerge when older ones fail.
    3. **Mathematics covers only part of experience**; ethics, aesthetics, and politics lie outside its scope.
    4. **Evolution may have primed humans for mathematics**, making long chains of reasoning possible.
  - He also gives specific examples involving:
    - Galileo’s falling-body reasoning
    - the inverse-square law and spatial dimensionality
    - the uncertainty principle and Fourier integrals
    - Einstein’s special relativity as “scholastic” theorizing guided by mathematical structure

- **Max Tegmark’s view**
  - Tegmark argues that physics works because the physical world **is** a mathematical structure.
  - Our theories succeed because they are **simpler mathematics approximating more complex mathematics**.
  - The exact mathematical structure corresponding to our world, if it exists, has not yet been found.

- **Ivor Grattan-Guinness’s critique**
  - He says the effectiveness is not mysterious but can be explained through **analogy, generalization, and metaphor**.
  - He also notes that Wigner overlooks the reverse direction: **science has often motivated mathematics**.

- **Michael Atiyah’s inversion**
  - Atiyah flips Wigner’s idea with **“the unreasonable effectiveness of physics in mathematics.”**
  - He argues physics tools helped mathematicians, especially in work related to **4-manifolds** and **Edward Witten**.
  - Mentions physics concepts such as **quantum field theory, special relativity, gauge theory, spin, chirality, supersymmetry, and electromagnetic duality**.

- **Earlier antecedents**
  - The article mentions **Moritz Drobisch**, who praised the “mathematical fundament” of sciences and saw cosmic order as evidence of profound wisdom.
  - This places Wigner’s theme in a broader philosophical tradition about order, mathematics, and nature.
