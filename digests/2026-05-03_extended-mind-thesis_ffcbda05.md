### TL;DR
Wikipedia’s **extended mind thesis** article explains Andy Clark and David Chalmers’ 1998 claim that cognition can literally extend into the environment via tools like notebooks or computers, then lays out the Otto/Inga thought experiment, major philosophical criticisms, and its links to embodied/enacted cognition.

### Key Quote
"the mind and the environment act as a "coupled system" that can be seen as a complete cognitive system of its own."

### Summary
- **Topic:** Philosophy of mind / cognitive science claim that mental processes are not confined to the brain or body.
- **Core thesis:** Some external objects can be part of a cognitive process when they function as active components of cognition, especially when they store information and are reliably used like internal memory.
- **Origin:** Proposed by **Andy Clark and David Chalmers** in **“The Extended Mind” (1998)**.
- **Key framing:** They describe the idea as **“active externalism”**—the environment is not just a trigger for thought but can help constitute it.

- **Central example: Otto and Inga**
  - **Inga** recalls museum directions from internal memory.
  - **Otto**, who has Alzheimer’s, writes the directions in a notebook and consults it as needed.
  - The argument is that Otto’s notebook can count as part of his memory system because it is:
    - **constantly and immediately accessible**
    - **automatically endorsed**
    - used in a way functionally parallel to internal memory
  - Clark and Chalmers compare the notebook to a **“fragile biological limb or organ”** that Otto protects as part of himself.

- **Main criterion in the original paper:** external objects count as part of an extended cognitive system when they perform the **same functional role** as internal processes in guiding cognition.

- **Later research / related extension:**
  - The excerpt includes a **less central, later research-oriented section** drawing on **shared intentionality** and **Igor Val Danilov**.
  - It claims group dynamics and, more controversially, **mother–fetus neurocognitive coupling** could support an extended-mind-style interpretation.
  - This section cites fetal-response and MRI findings (including responses at **33 weeks of gestation**) and argues that maternal acoustic/environmental signals may help shape fetal cognition.
  - In the context of this article, this reads as a **niche, contested add-on**, not the core canonical formulation of the thesis.

- **Criticisms listed in the article:**
  - **Causal-constitutional fallacy:** confuses things that merely influence cognition with things that constitute cognition.
  - **Cognitive bloating:** if taken too far, the thesis could imply absurdly broad cognitive systems, even “everything on the Internet.”
  - **Overly coarse functionalism:** critics argue it blurs important differences between internal mental states and external tools.
  - The article notes Clark’s response: coupling is **necessary but not sufficient**; not every coupled object is an extension of mind.
  - Some later reformulations shift from strong ontological claims toward **complementarity** and explanatory usefulness for cognitive science.
  - **Vincent C. Müller** is quoted as arguing the thesis may reduce to a verbal issue about where to draw the boundary of the “mental.”

- **Relation to 4E cognition:**
  - The article places the thesis alongside:
    - **Embodied** cognition
    - **Embedded** cognition
    - **Enacted** cognition
    - **Extended** cognition
  - It presents extended cognition as the view that cognition involves interaction with the environment, not just internal neural computation.
  - Example given: a spider web may function partly as sensory and cognitive support.
