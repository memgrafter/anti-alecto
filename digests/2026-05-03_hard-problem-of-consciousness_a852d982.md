### TL;DR
Wikipedia’s article on the **hard problem of consciousness** explains David Chalmers’ distinction between “easy” functional problems of mind and the alleged irreducible question of why physical processes are accompanied by subjective experience, then surveys the major pro- and anti-hard-problem positions in philosophy and neuroscience.

### Key Quote
“why is the performance of these functions accompanied by experience?”

### Summary
- **Core definition**
  - In philosophy of mind, the hard problem is explaining **why and how organisms have qualia, phenomenal consciousness, or subjective experience**.
  - It is contrasted with “easy problems,” which concern functions like perception, discrimination, integration of information, speech, and behavior.
  - Easy problems are treated as **functional/mechanistic** and explainable by structure and dynamics; the hard problem is claimed to be **categorically different**.

- **Chalmers’ formulation and origin**
  - The terms **“hard problem”** and **“easy problems”** were coined by **David Chalmers** in a **1994 talk** and developed in his **1995 paper** “Facing up to the problem of consciousness” and his **1996 book** *The Conscious Mind*.
  - His basic challenge: even after all cognitive and behavioral functions are explained, there remains the question of **why those functions are accompanied by experience**.
  - Chalmers’ use of “easy” is explicitly **tongue-in-cheek**.

- **Easy vs. hard problems**
  - Easy problems: sensory processing, verbal report, thought, emotion, and other neural/behavioral mechanisms.
  - Hard problem: why there is **something it is like** to be a system, and why a given physical process has a **particular qualitative feel**.
  - The article repeatedly uses examples like **pain**, **hunger**, and **color experience** to show the gap Chalmers thinks remains after physical explanation.

- **Why Chalmers thinks it matters**
  - If the hard problem is real, then **physicalism/materialism is false**.
  - Chalmers rejects physicalism but still describes himself as a **naturalist**.
  - The article also notes related arguments from **Christian List** about first-person perspective and the limits of physicalism.

- **Historical precedents**
  - The article says Chalmers’ formulation has antecedents in thinkers such as **Newton, Locke, Leibniz, Mill, Huxley**, and also Asian philosophers like **Dharmakirti** and **Guifeng Zongmi**.
  - It frames Chalmers as giving an old family of problems a memorable label, not inventing the concern from scratch.

- **Related philosophical arguments**
  - **Nagel’s “What Is It Like to Be a Bat?”**: subjective experience resists objective reduction.
  - **Levine’s explanatory gap**: even if consciousness is physical, the bridge from physical states to experience remains contingent and unexplained.
  - **Philosophical zombies**: physically identical beings without experience are used to argue experience is not logically entailed by physical facts.
  - **Mary’s Room / knowledge argument**: knowing all physical facts still may not reveal what red “looks like.”
  - The article presents these as major supports for the hard-problem intuition.

- **Main dispute: does the hard problem exist?**
  - **Accept it**: philosophers/neuroscientists such as **Joseph Levine, Colin McGinn, Ned Block, Francisco Varela, Giulio Tononi, Christof Koch**.
  - **Reject it**: **Dennett, Patricia Churchland, Thomas Metzinger, Keith Frankish, Stanislas Dehaene, Bernard Baars, Anil Seth, Antonio Damasio**, among others.
  - The article cites a **2020 PhilPapers survey**: **62.4%** of philosophers said the hard problem is a genuine problem; **29.7%** said it does not exist.

- **Taxonomy of responses**
  - **Type-A materialism**: the hard problem is illusory or reducible; includes **reductive materialism** and **illusionism**.
  - **Type-B materialism**: physicalism is true, but our intuitions create an explanatory gap; often tied to **phenomenal concepts** and a distinction between **hard-wired** and **soft-wired** versions.
  - **Type-C materialism**: the gap is due to ignorance and will eventually be resolved by science.
  - **Type-D / Type-E dualism**: consciousness is non-physical or involves non-physical properties; includes **interactionism** and **epiphenomenalism**.
  - **Type-F monism**: views like **panpsychism** and **neutral monism**, which treat consciousness as intrinsic to matter.
  - The article also discusses **idealism/cosmopsychism** and **new mysterianism**.

- **Important anti-hard-problem positions**
  - **Strong reductionism**: consciousness is real but fully explainable in functional/brain terms.
  - **Illusionism**: phenomenal consciousness is an illusion; the task is explaining why it seems to exist.
  - **Dennett** is presented as a major defender, using arguments like **change blindness** and critiques of introspective certainty.
  - Critics of illusionism are said to rely on **Moorean arguments**: consciousness is more certain than any theory denying it.

- **Scientific frameworks discussed**
  - **Neural correlates of consciousness (NCCs)**: identify what brain events accompany consciousness, but may not answer why experience exists at all.
  - **Computational cognition**: treats cognition and possibly consciousness as computation.
  - **Integrated Information Theory (IIT)**: identifies consciousness with integrated information (Φ); proponents think it may help solve the hard problem, while critics say it addresses only a “Pretty Hard Problem.”
  - **Global Workspace Theory (GWT)**: explains global accessibility of information, but critics say it does not explain subjective experience.
  - **Representationalism**: attempts to bridge philosophy of mind and neuroscience via mental representation.

- **Meta-problem**
  - In **2018**, Chalmers proposed the **meta-problem of consciousness**: explaining why we think there is a hard problem.
  - He treats this as an “easy problem” that may shed light on, constrain, or possibly even dissolve the hard problem.

- **Overall stance of the article**
  - The article is neutral and survey-like, but it clearly presents the hard problem as a major live debate in philosophy of mind.
  - It emphasizes that the dispute is not just about consciousness itself, but about whether subjective experience can ever be fully captured by functional or physical explanation.
