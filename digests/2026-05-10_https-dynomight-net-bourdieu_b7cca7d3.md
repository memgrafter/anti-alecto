### TL;DR
Dynomight argues that Pierre Bourdieu’s *Distinction* is a painfully written but important theory of how class shapes taste, how taste signals class, and how these signals help reproduce social inequality.

### Key Quote
“**Tastes classifies, and it classifies the classifier.**”

### Summary
- **What this post is**
  - A grumbling, highly informal abridgment of Bourdieu’s 1979 book *Distinction: A Social Critique of the Judgement of Taste*.
  - The author uses personal examples (beer, books, movies, words, furniture) to explain why taste seems class-coded.

- **Central complaint about Bourdieu’s writing**
  - The book is described as extremely hard to read:
    - very long sentences
    - unclear organization
    - new terms introduced without definition
    - normal words redefined without warning
  - The author does not accept Bourdieu’s implied defense that the obscure style is necessary to express social complexity.
  - The style is framed as creating unnecessary mental labor and making the book feel self-important.

- **The author’s seven-part simplified version of Bourdieu’s theory**
  - **1. Class predicts taste**
    - Rich educated people and poor uneducated people tend to prefer different foods, books, clothes, movies, hobbies, music, etc.
    - This is presented as true in Bourdieu’s France and likely still true in the Anglosphere.
  - **2. Taste predicts class**
    - Knowing what someone likes can reveal their class position or at least strongly suggest it.
    - Taste is a class signal, not just a random personal quirk.
  - **3. Taste has consequences**
    - People prefer, trust, and hire those who seem culturally similar.
    - “Cultural fit” and social networks help convert taste into jobs and opportunities.
  - **4. People respond to these incentives**
    - People adjust tastes because social rewards are attached to them.
  - **5. Much of this happens unconsciously**
    - The author frames Bourdieu’s view as: people do not consciously optimize taste, but internalize incentives and preferences.
  - **6. Acquiring “the right” tastes is difficult**
    - Outsiders to elite culture can expose themselves by making mistakes.
    - Belonging to the class/culture is required to fully perform it.
  - **7. Taste helps entrench class**
    - Early exposure to elite culture creates a feedback loop:
      - better taste recognition
      - more acceptance in upper-class circles
      - more social and economic opportunities
    - Those without early access are disadvantaged and may remain excluded.

- **“Legitimate culture”**
  - Bourdieu’s framework treats elite culture—opera, Bach, avant-garde theater, fine art, skiing, golf, etc.—as “legitimate.”
  - Schools and institutions transmit the idea that this culture is inherently better.
  - Upper-class students benefit because they already have exposure at home.
  - The author is skeptical that this mechanism generalizes neatly to the present-day Anglosphere, where high-status culture may be less centered on canonical “legitimate” art and more on style, brands, music, and social signaling.

- **Analogy to language**
  - The author draws a parallel between “legitimate” culture and standard language:
    - dialects like Black English or Cockney are socially stigmatized even though they are linguistically valid
    - schools reward the language of higher-status homes
  - The post notes that Bourdieu does not seem to emphasize this analogy, despite his own background speaking Gascon dialect.

- **Abstraction vs. omnivores**
  - Bourdieu’s theory says upper-class people prefer abstraction, form, and “pure” appreciation rather than straightforward content.
  - The author cites Bourdieu’s survey examples about whether photos of certain subjects are “beautiful,” “meaningless,” etc.
  - But later sociology proposes the **cultural omnivore** idea:
    - high-status people increasingly like many genres, cuisines, and activities
    - status is shown by range and flexibility, not narrow elite taste
  - The author thinks this better matches the 2020s, though Bourdieu may still have captured an earlier regime.

- **Main evaluation of Bourdieu’s value**
  - The author is skeptical of some specifics, especially whether today’s upper-class signaling still revolves around “legitimate culture” in the old sense.
  - Still, the author finds Bourdieu’s theory more compelling than modern “everyone is oppressed by everything” discourse because:
    - it argues from first principles
    - it targets a real mainstream status order
    - it explains concrete social mechanisms
  - The post ends by saying that later institutionalized versions of these ideas in HR/DEI/PR are less charming than Bourdieu’s original confrontational version.
