### TL;DR
Dynomight argues that the “data wall” objection to AI progress is weak because humans are not a good analogy for data efficiency: our intelligence is shaped by billions of years of evolutionary information, embodied biological machinery, and a possibly opaque learning algorithm, so human examples don’t prove AI will run out of useful training data.

### Key Quote
> “I’m just saying that if you want to argue for visual data or algorithmic progress or whatever, a direct argument is more convincing than gesturing at some human babies.”

### Summary
- **Topic / thesis**
  - The post challenges the common claim that AI will hit a “data wall” because humans learn language and reason from far less data than modern language models.
  - The author’s core position: human intelligence is not a clean counterexample to data-hungry AI, because humans are shaped by vastly more information than just lifetime text exposure.

- **Why humans are not a useful comparison**
  - A typical person may encounter on the order of **5×10^8 words** in a lifetime.
  - Modern language models are trained on roughly **10^12 to 10^13 words**, which is about **200,000× more**.
  - The author argues that comparing AI training data to a human’s lifetime language exposure ignores other information sources:
    - **Vision** and **sound** may matter, but the post argues they are not obviously the “secret sauce.”
    - Examples invoked include **blind people**, **deaf people**, **CIPA / touch-insensitive individuals**, and **deafblind people** with early access to language.
  - Main point: humans are too biologically special and varied for this analogy to settle what AI needs.

- **Evolution as a hidden information source**
  - The post argues humans are born with **extremely sophisticated “programming”** from evolution.
  - This evolutionary heritage may encode information accumulated over **millions to billions of years**, not just what a person learns after birth.
  - The author uses an information-theory comparison:
    - Human DNA: about **2.9 billion base pairs**, roughly **6 billion bits** in a simplified estimate.
    - A lifetime of human language (using estimated bits/word) may contain around **1.2 billion bits** of information.
  - Conclusion: DNA may contain as much or more information than a person’s lifetime language exposure.

- **“Embodied information” beyond DNA**
  - The author says DNA is not the whole story; there is also information embodied in:
    - cell structure
    - molecular machinery
    - the physical mechanisms that interpret DNA
  - The analogy used: a compiler written in a language cannot run itself unless something already knows how to execute it.
  - This is meant to show that biology includes a lot of inherited structure that is not captured by a simplistic “DNA bytes count.”

- **Double-loop optimization framing**
  - Human intelligence is described as a **double-loop optimization**:
    - **Outer loop:** evolution searches over learning strategies across generations
    - **Inner loop:** an individual learns during a lifetime
  - AI is described similarly:
    - **Outer loop:** engineers try different model/training ideas
    - **Inner loop:** the model trains on data
  - Key difference: evolution’s outer loop has run **many more times**, each time on fresh data, potentially producing a deeply tuned learning system.
  - This makes it plausible that human learning efficiency reflects not just data exposure but a long optimization process.

- **Why this still doesn’t prove AI will stall**
  - The author is careful not to claim a data wall definitely won’t happen.
  - Instead, the argument is narrower:
    - Human babies are not strong evidence that AI can’t benefit from more data or other modalities.
    - Claims about visual data or algorithmic advances should be argued directly, not inferred from human development.

- **Stance and tone**
  - The piece is skeptical of simplistic “humans learn from little data, so AI can too” reasoning.
  - It is also skeptical of hand-wavy optimism that says AI will escape the data wall because humans use vision or biology does something clever.
  - The tone is informal, playful, and somewhat combative toward weak analogies.
