### TL;DR
Rolling Stone’s obituary for **Bob Weir** says the Grateful Dead co-founder and rhythm guitarist died at **78** after cancer treatment and lung issues, and frames him as the often-underappreciated but essential musical force whose songs, stage role, and post-Garcia projects helped sustain the Dead’s legacy for six decades.

### Key Quote
“**Bobby will forever be a guiding force whose unique artistry reshaped American music,**” the statement added.

### Summary
- **What happened**
  - Bob Weir, singer, songwriter, guitarist, and co-founder of the **Grateful Dead**, died at **age 78**.
  - His family said he “transitioned peacefully, surrounded by loved ones,” after “courageously beating cancer,” but ultimately “succumbed to underlying lung issues.”
  - The article is an obituary that looks back on his life, musicianship, and role in keeping the Dead’s music alive after Jerry Garcia’s death.

- **Why Weir mattered**
  - Weir was the Dead’s **co-lead singer, writer, and rhythm guitarist** alongside Garcia.
  - The piece emphasizes that he was often **under-recognized** compared with Garcia, but was crucial to the band’s identity.
  - Phil Lesh is quoted describing Weir’s contribution as “**a stealth machine**,” meaning an understated but powerful presence.

- **Early life and formation of the band**
  - Born **Robert Hall Weir** in **San Francisco on Oct. 16, 1947** and adopted.
  - Raised in a Bay Area suburb; had **undiagnosed dyslexia** and was expelled from preschool and Cub Scouts.
  - At **Fountain Valley**, a Colorado school for boys with behavioral problems, he met **John Perry Barlow**, who became his most frequent lyricist.
  - Started guitar at **13**; played around the Palo Alto folk scene and first encountered Garcia at **Dana Morgan’s Music Store** on **New Year’s Eve, 1965**.
  - Their early bands — **Mother McCree’s Uptown Jug Champions** and then the **Warlocks** — became the **Grateful Dead**.

- **Role in the Grateful Dead**
  - Weir’s guitar style is described as **elliptical, eccentric, quirky, whimsical, and goofy**.
  - He often sang the band’s **country covers** and his own songs, helping define the Dead’s mix of **country-rock warmth** and improvisational freedom.
  - He wrote or helped popularize major Dead songs including:
    - “**The Other One**”
    - “**Playing in the Band**”
    - “**One More Saturday Night**”
    - “**Cassidy**”
    - “**Weather Report Suite**”
  - The article notes that in the band’s early years he was sometimes “low man on the totem pole,” especially under the influence-heavy environment of the **Acid Tests**.

- **Beyond the Dead**
  - After **Pigpen’s death in 1972**, Weir became the band’s second major vocalist more fully.
  - His solo album **Ace** established him as a strong songwriter outside the Dead.
  - He played with and formed side projects including:
    - **Kingfish** (1974)
    - **Bob Weir Band**
    - **Bobby and the Midnites**
    - later solo work including **Heaven Help the Fool** (1978) and **Blue Mountain** (2016)

- **After Jerry Garcia**
  - Following Garcia’s death in **1995**, Weir kept performing through projects like:
    - **RatDog**
    - **The Other Ones**
    - **The Dead**
    - **Furthur**
  - He also admitted at one point to struggling with **painkiller addiction**.
  - The article says he even served as a “**bag man**” for Garcia’s drugs during the 1980s.

- **Dead & Company and final years**
  - In **2015**, Weir supported a Dead reunion and later joined **Dead & Company** with **John Mayer**, **Mickey Hart**, **Bill Kreutzmann**, and others.
  - Dead & Company carried the Dead’s legacy through a **2023 Final Tour** and performances at **Las Vegas’ Sphere**.
  - Weir’s **final performances** were a **three-night run at Golden Gate Park in San Francisco in August 2024** marking the band’s **60th anniversary**.
  - The obituary says he was diagnosed in **July 2024** and returned to his hometown stage only weeks later.

- **Tone and legacy**
  - The family statement frames Weir’s life as one of **resilience**, **community**, and a “300-year legacy” meant to outlast him.
  - The article closes by casting his death not as an ending but as part of the continuing Grateful Dead mythology and fan culture.
