### TL;DR
A polemical AI essay arguing that software development has become so cheap and accessible that “software developer” is no longer a durable identity, because model-first companies, faster upskilling, and AI-driven automation are collapsing margins, org charts, and old moats.

### Key Quote
“the cost of software development is $10.42 an hour, which is less than minimum wage and a burger flipper at macca's gets paid more than that.”

### Summary
- The piece argues that recent AI model improvements have made software development dramatically cheaper and more accessible, to the point that non-developers at a Cursor meetup were building real things and effectively becoming software developers.
- The author frames this as a **commoditisation of software development skill**: if everyone can build software, then being “a software developer” stops being a strong identity function or economic moat.
- The essay repeatedly contrasts:
  - **Model-first companies**: lean, fast, able to operate on razor-thin margins.
  - **Traditional companies**: forced into “people transformation,” AI adoption programs, and org redesign.
- It claims the timeline for competitive pressure has compressed from years to **months**, because AI-enabled competitors can build, iterate, and attack incumbents much faster.
- Employees are urged to **upskill in AI** or risk “employment suicide,” while the author says companies that resist AI create talent-flight risk.
- The author draws a strong line between:
  - **Company problem**: an employer failing to adopt AI.
  - **Employee problem**: an individual failing to adapt and stay relevant.
- A major theme is that classic business moats are weakening:
  - **Per-seat pricing** is under pressure as AI-first firms use utility-style pricing.
  - **Human-designed product features/platforms** can now be cloned quickly with Claude Code and screenshots.
  - **Switching costs / migration lock-in** are less defensible because AI can automate migrations.
- The author gives personal examples of rebuilding or cloning tools like **Posthog, Jira, Pipedrive, and Calendly**, and mentions migrating from **Cloudflare D1 to PlanetScale Postgres** via a “Ralph Loop.”
- The essay’s revised definition of moat emphasizes:
  - **Distribution**
  - **Brand awareness**
  - **Utility-based pricing**
  - **Operating as a model-first company**
- It predicts org charts will flatten sharply; many management layers become less valuable as AI reduces “Dilbert activities.”
- A quoted anonymous founder claims that by cutting headcount and refusing backfills, a much smaller team produced far more output, illustrating the essay’s thesis that fewer people are needed.
- The tone is urgent and confrontational: the author says AI is being “rammed into the world non-consentually,” that some careers will be disrupted hard, and that “there’s no going back.”
- Distinctive phrases and examples that help identify the piece include:
  - “burger flipper at macca’s”
  - “steaks and handshakes”
  - “rip a fart into Claude Code”
  - “AI is burrowing under their house”
