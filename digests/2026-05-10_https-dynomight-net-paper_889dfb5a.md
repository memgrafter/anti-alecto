### TL;DR
Dynomight argues that paper is underrated for thinking, not storage, and explains how he settled on a simple three-hole-punched, chronological paper system after trying notebooks and notecards.

### Key Quote
“Optimize for thinking.”

### Summary
- **Main thesis:** Paper’s main value is that it improves thinking by making it easier to sketch, scribble, and work through ideas.
- **Core argument:** Paper is usually a poor storage system:
  - hard to search
  - cross-references are weak
  - copying and modifying are inconvenient
  - old notes are rarely revisited
- **Practical lesson:** Don’t optimize paper for archiving; optimize it for the act of thinking.

- **Observation 2: Systems matter**
  - If paper use is frictionless, you’ll use it more.
  - A “system” can be very simple:
    - pick paper
    - decide where it lives when done
    - reduce setup friction
  - Without this, even beneficial paper use gets abandoned.

- **Observation 3: User experience matters**
  - Pens and paper differ in how enjoyable they are.
  - Better-feeling tools lead to better thinking.
  - The author dislikes lined paper and notes that notebook design flaws (closures, crushed spirals/wires) reduce the joy of using them.

- **Observation 4: Categorization is hard**
  - Strict notebook categories (“work,” “hobby,” “journal”) sound neat but break down in practice.
  - Real notes often cut across categories and create edge cases.
  - Managing multiple notebooks becomes annoying and kills the joy of paper.

- **Paper systems the author tried**
  - **College system:** stolen printer paper, scribbled on, then left in messy stacks; surprisingly workable in hindsight.
  - **Notebooks:** rejected because of categorization problems, ugly crossed-out draft material, and lack of visible-at-once spread.
  - **Notecards:** appealing because they lower the threshold to write and are easy to rearrange/discard, but too small for substantial writing and eventually become piles that are hard to manage.

- **Current preferred system**
  - Buy **three-hole-punched printer paper**
  - Write on it
  - Keep everything in **one three-ring notebook**, strictly in chronological order
  - When full:
    - remove the pages
    - add **brown cardstock** at both ends
    - bind with **brass fasteners**
    - shelve it and never look at it again
  - This is the first system the author says he actually likes.

- **Tone / style**
  - Light, personal, and intentionally mundane.
  - The post is less a formal essay than a set of lived-in observations about how paper affects thinking and workflow.
