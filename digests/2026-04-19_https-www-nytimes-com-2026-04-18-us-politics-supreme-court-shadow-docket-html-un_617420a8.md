### TL;DR
The New York Times says newly obtained internal Supreme Court memos show that the modern “shadow docket” began with a rushed, partisan 2016 decision to block Obama’s Clean Power Plan, revealing how emergency rulings became a routine and consequential way for the Court to reshape policy.

### Key Quote
“we’ve done it to ourselves,”

### Summary
- This April 18, 2026 New York Times investigation, written by Jodi Kantor and Adam Liptak, is based on **16 pages of internal Supreme Court memos** exchanged over **five days in February 2016**.
- The article argues that a **one-paragraph emergency order** blocking President Barack Obama’s **Clean Power Plan** was the moment that effectively launched the Court’s modern **“shadow docket”**—a fast, largely unexplained emergency track now used for major rulings.
- The memos show the justices debating the request to stay the rule **outside public view**, using first names and initials, and citing unusual materials like a **blog post** and a **BBC interview**.
- Chief Justice **John G. Roberts Jr.** pushed aggressively for immediate intervention, arguing:
  - the rule would force irreversible changes in the power sector,
  - the case would eventually be overturned anyway,
  - the costs to states and industry could be enormous,
  - and the EPA had tried to sideline the Court after earlier mercury-emissions litigation.
- Justice **Elena Kagan** objected that emergency intervention was **“unprecedented”** and that the thin briefing made it hard to know who would ultimately win.
- Justice **Samuel Alito** argued that not stopping the rule would threaten the Court’s **“institutional legitimacy.”**
- The vote split largely along ideological lines:
  - Roberts, Alito, Scalia, Thomas, and Kennedy favored the stay.
  - Breyer, Ginsburg, Sotomayor, and Kagan opposed it.
- **Justice Anthony Kennedy** appears to have been the decisive vote; he sided with the chief justice in a short memo, saying the Court would likely stay the plan soon anyway.
- The piece emphasizes that **none of the justices mentioned climate change itself** as a harm in the private correspondence; their focus was on procedure, timing, and economic burden.
- The article places the order in the context of the Court’s broader shift:
  - in public, the stay looked like a temporary procedural move,
  - but in retrospect it marked the start of a **new normal** in which emergency applications became frequent and often highly consequential.
- The article notes that the next few days changed everything:
  - **Justice Scalia died** days later, which would have made the Clean Power Plan case deadlock if the Court had delayed.
  - **Donald Trump’s election** later that year rendered the Obama rule moot in practice.
  - In **2022**, the Court later held, through normal procedures, that the Clean Air Act did not authorize such sweeping power-sector climate regulation.
- The article closes by arguing that today’s flood of emergency applications is partly driven by politics and executive overreach, but also by the Court’s own decision to accept and normalize this kind of rushed decision-making.
- Final note: **Justice Sonia Sotomayor** is quoted saying of the emergency docket, **“We’ve done it to ourselves.”**
