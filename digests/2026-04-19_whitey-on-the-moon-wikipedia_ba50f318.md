### TL;DR
Gil Scott-Heron’s 1970 spoken-word poem “Whitey on the Moon” links the Apollo era’s triumphal space narrative to Black poverty, medical debt, and government neglect, and the Wikipedia article traces its origins, reception, and later revivals in film, TV, and 2021 commentary.

### Key Quote
“Ten years from now I'll be paying still. / (while Whitey's on the Moon)”

### Summary
- **What it is**
  - “Whitey on the Moon” is a **spoken-word poem** by **Gil Scott-Heron**.
  - It was released as the **ninth track** on his debut album **_Small Talk at 125th and Lenox_** in **1970**.
  - The track is **just under two minutes long** and features **conga drum accompaniment**.

- **What it says**
  - The poem narrates the experience of Scott-Heron’s fictionalized “**sister Nell**” being bitten by a rat while **Neil Armstrong lands on the Moon**.
  - It emphasizes **medical debt**, **rising costs**, **high taxes**, and **poverty**.
  - Its central critique is that the U.S. spent heavily on the **space program** while **Black Americans** faced social and economic hardship at home.
  - The closing bitter joke is that future bills would be sent by **“air mail special to Whitey on the Moon.”**

- **Origins and recording**
  - Scott-Heron said he was inspired by **Eldridge Cleaver’s** argument that the space program distracted from domestic problems and suppressed discontent.
  - He wrote the poem in the **summer of 1969**.
  - His mother, **Bobbie Scott**, suggested the refrain and closing line.
  - Although the album was often described as recorded live in a nightclub, the article says it was actually recorded in a **studio** with an **audience present** to simulate a live crowd.

- **Reception and significance**
  - The poem became especially popular among **African-American listeners** in inner-city neighborhoods in **New York, Detroit, and Los Angeles**.
  - It is described as aligning with broader Black critiques of the **Space Race** and as evidence of growing awareness of **urban blight**.
  - The article frames it as an example of **afrofuturism** and as part of Scott-Heron’s wider critique linking **capitalism**, **poverty**, **environmental destruction**, and **militarism**.
  - Commentators praise its **dark humor** and its ability to challenge celebratory moon-landing narratives without becoming heavy-handed.

- **Later appearances and renewed relevance**
  - The poem was prominently featured in the 2018 film **_First Man_**, where it is used over scenes involving **Apollo 1** and protests against the space program.
  - It appears in the second episode of HBO’s **_Lovecraft Country_**, titled **“Whitey’s on the Moon”**, which aired on **August 23, 2020**.
  - It gained renewed attention in **July 2021** after the spaceflights of **Jeff Bezos** and **Richard Branson**, with commentators using it to critique billionaire space tourism and inequality.
  - On **April 17, 2023**, NASA astronaut **Victor Glover** said he listens to it every Monday as a reminder that people in communities like the one he grew up in “didn't feel heard.”
