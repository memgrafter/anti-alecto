### TL;DR
The author argues that “harness engineering” is mostly a new label for long-standing software-engineering ideas, and uses their own SGLang support system to show that better agent environments—not bigger models—were what actually improved performance.

### Key Quote
“None of this is new.”

### Summary
- The post is a reaction to a long essay on “Harness Engineering,” which the author suspects is largely AI-written and represents a familiar pattern in AI culture: renaming existing ideas with new jargon.
- Their core complaint is that terms like **prompt engineering**, **context engineering**, and now **harness engineering** repackage established engineering practices:
  - designing the model’s environment
  - controlling what information it sees
  - limiting tools and scope
  - managing memory and errors
- The author argues this is not a new discipline, but rather ordinary engineering applied to LLM systems.

- They then use **how-to-sglang** as a concrete example:
  - SGLang gets many complex support questions, such as:
    - deploying **DeepSeek-V3 on 8 GPUs**
    - fixing **gateway-to-worker address** issues
    - comparing **GLM-5 INT4** vs official **FP8**
  - A naive attempt to build one all-knowing agent over all docs/code failed because the **context window isn’t RAM** and attention becomes diluted.
  - The solution was a **multi-layered sub-domain expert architecture**:
    - separate expert agents for natural functional boundaries
    - an **Expert Debating Manager** to decompose questions
    - an **Expert Routing Table** to choose which agents to activate
    - parallel solving and synthesis of answers

- The author maps this design to the supposedly new “harness engineering” principles, but says they discovered them through trial and error rather than theory:
  1. **Progressive disclosure**
     - each agent loads only its own domain knowledge
     - the manager activates experts based on question type
     - this mattered more than upgrading to a stronger model
  2. **Repository as source of truth**
     - all knowledge lives in markdown files inside the repo
     - they tried a single massive `sglang-maintain.md`, found it stale and unworkable
     - they cite OpenAI’s **Codex** team making a similar mistake with an oversized `AGENTS.md`
  3. **Structured routing**
     - question types map explicitly to expert agents
     - e.g. a **GLM-5 INT4** question activates both the **Cookbook Domain Expert** and **Quantization Domain Expert**
     - the manager follows an index rather than improvising

- The author says the cited research is still useful:
  - **SWE-agent’s ACI concept** is worth knowing
  - **Anthropic’s dual-agent architecture** (initializer agent + coding agent) is a valuable reference
- But they dislike the trend of inventing new labels and making people feel behind if they don’t know the latest term.

- Their broader takeaway:
  - keep information **minimal and precise**
  - split systems into **specialized sub-modules**
  - keep all knowledge **in the repo**
  - enforce **structural routing and constraints**
  - tighten feedback loops
  - use logging and **Codex as an LLM-as-a-judge**
- They frame these as standard engineering concepts:
  - separation of concerns
  - single responsibility principle
  - docs-as-code
  - shift-left constraints

- The post ends with an open question:
  - if model capabilities keep scaling, might models eventually build their own environments?
  - They mention **OpenClaw**, which reportedly grew from **400K lines to 1M lines in a month** driven entirely by AI
  - The author isn’t sure how many of today’s “harness” principles will matter in two years
  - For now, they believe environment design is still fundamentally human work and the most valuable kind
