A viral X thread on Marvin Minsky argues, using *The Society of Mind*, that human intelligence is a “parliament” of competing mental agents rather than a single rational self, and turns that idea into a productivity takeaway: change the environment so the right agents win.

- The post frames Marvin Minsky as a major early AI thinker:
  - Co-founded MIT’s artificial intelligence lab with John McCarthy in 1959
  - Built SNARC in 1951, described here as the first randomly wired neural network learning machine
  - Won the Turing Award
  - Advised Stanley Kubrick on *2001: A Space Odyssey*
  - Isaac Asimov is quoted as saying Minsky was one of only two people he would admit were more intelligent than him
- The core claim from *The Society of Mind*:
  - “you do not have a mind. You have thousands of them.”
  - Human thought is presented as the output of many specialized, mostly mindless agents competing, negotiating, and cooperating below conscious awareness
  - Conscious unity is described as an outcome, not a controller
- The thread emphasizes Minsky’s line that:
  - “in general, we are least aware of what our minds do best.”
  - The easiest-feeling human abilities, like reading faces, walking through crowds, understanding language, and catching balls, are actually the products of very complex underlying systems
  - By contrast, deliberate reasoning and planning are portrayed as the small, awkward surface layer we can notice
- The productivity implication is explicit:
  - If decisions emerge from whichever mental coalition wins in the moment, then self-improvement is less about “becoming more disciplined” and more about shaping conditions so desired outcomes happen automatically
  - Examples given:
    - removing your phone from the room works better than relying on resolve
    - writing one task on an index card can beat a complex system
    - commitment devices outperform motivation
- The thread’s closing metaphor:
  - the human mind is “a city,” loud and chaotic, full of competing interests, with no mayor
  - people who understand this stop trying to brute-force willpower and instead “build a better city”
