### TL;DR
Joan Westenberg argues that modern life is dominated by “thin desires” — easy, addictive, low-transformation satisfactions — and makes the case for deliberately pursuing “thick desires” like baking, handwriting letters, and learning craft, which take time and change you.

### Key Quote
“A thick desire is one that changes you in the process of pursuing it.”

### Summary
- The essay’s central distinction is between:
  - **Thick desires**: wants that transform the person pursuing them
  - **Thin desires**: wants that give quick satisfaction but leave the person unchanged
- Examples used to contrast the two:
  - **Thick**: learning calculus, mastering a craft, slow reading, community, tradition, baking bread, writing handwritten letters, building software for one person
  - **Thin**: checking notifications, social media connection without friendship, pornography without partnership, productivity apps that feel productive without producing results
- Westenberg argues that consumer technology is designed to:
  - isolate the rewarding part of a thick desire
  - deliver that reward cheaply and at scale
  - remove the obligations, effort, and transformation that make the desire meaningful
- She links the spread of thin desires to broader cultural symptoms:
  - rising anxiety
  - rising depression
  - rising loneliness despite greater connectivity
- Her diagnosis is not just moral panic about technology; she explicitly warns against reducing the argument to “Kids These Days” grumbling.
- She argues that thick desires are structurally inconvenient in a frictionless, scalable marketplace because they:
  - take years to cultivate
  - cannot be satisfied on demand
  - create dependence, obligation, and reciprocity
  - require specific people, places, and traditions
- The essay says the infrastructure supporting thick desires has been weakened:
  - workshops closed
  - congregations thinned
  - apprenticeships disappeared
  - front porches gave way to decks, studio apartments, and “Micro Homes”
- Meanwhile, the infrastructure for thin desires is ubiquitous and inseparable from modern life, especially via devices “in your pocket.”
- The practical response is not a grand movement to “restore meaning,” which the author thinks may not scale, but small acts of resistance:
  - **Bake bread** because it cannot be optimized away
  - **Write letters by hand** because they resist metrics and instant feedback
  - **Code for one person** because not everything needs to scale or be monetized
- The essay’s final point is modest and personal:
  - the thick life may not “fix the world”
  - but it can prevent you from feeling emptied out
  - and it can reconnect you with wanting something genuinely worth wanting
