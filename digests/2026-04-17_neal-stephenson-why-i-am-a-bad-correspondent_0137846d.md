### TL;DR
Neal Stephenson explains that he limits correspondence and speaking engagements because interruptions break the long, uninterrupted time he needs to write novels well, and preserving his fiction output is more important than replying to everyone.

### Key Quote
“Writing novels is hard, and requires vast, unbroken slabs of time.”

### Summary
- This is a short personal statement/form-letter from Neal Stephenson explaining why he often does not answer reader email or accept many speaking invitations.
- He says the common impulse to contact an admired author is understandable: readers feel a strong connection to a novel and want to extend that connection through direct interaction.
- He argues, however, that the experience of meeting or corresponding with an author is not the same as reading their work:
  - A novel is heavily edited, refined, and distilled from years of work.
  - Direct conversation is much less polished and often disappointing by comparison.
- Stephenson treats reader email and public speaking invitations as part of the same basic issue: both require direct interaction that takes time away from writing fiction.
- His main practical argument is about productivity:
  - He needs long, uninterrupted blocks of time to write effectively.
  - Short interruptions or fragmented time are much less useful than contiguous time.
  - Even if the total hours are the same, broken-up time is far less productive for him.
- He says his best contribution to readers is not individual correspondence but more fiction:
  - a novel reaches more people,
  - lasts longer,
  - and is of much higher quality than his email replies or speeches.
- He adds a mental-health argument: regular fiction writing helps keep him from becoming depressed and unpleasant to be around.
- He does not claim this is ideal or something to be proud of; he says unanswered email is regrettable and he is not trying to be rude.
- He closes by noting that if he were a commercial bestseller, he could hire staff to handle correspondence, but as things stand he must choose between being a “bad correspondent” and being a “good novelist.”
