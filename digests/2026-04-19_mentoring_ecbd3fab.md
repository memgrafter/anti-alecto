### TL;DR
A personal mentoring manifesto urging developers to build relationships in open source, own their online identity, choose boring tech, publish on their own domain, and treat community/communication as career-defining skills.

### Key Quote
“the standard pace is for chumps” — that the system is designed so anyone can keep up.

### Summary
- This page is an open invitation to contact the author for mentoring/catch-ups by email or calendar booking, but it asks readers to first read the advice below.
- The central message is that **open source and community connections changed the author’s career**, starting with a 2013 blog post by Brendan Forster that showed the value of having a human to ask open-source questions.
- The author argues that the best way to learn open source is not by joining the biggest projects, but by:
  - showing up consistently,
  - introducing yourself,
  - stating your intent to help,
  - asking for help,
  - documenting work,
  - and acting as the project’s “janitor.”
- There is a strong warning that **open source is structurally broken**:
  - much of the software infrastructure used by developers is maintained by unpaid volunteers,
  - companies rely on it without adequately supporting maintainers,
  - and this creates risk in security, uptime, and innovation.
- The author recommends that maintainers protect their well-being:
  - if open source stops being fun, stop doing it,
  - and remember that licenses explicitly say software is provided “AS IS” with no warranty.
- Career/identity advice includes:
  - **own your realname.com**,
  - keep domain registration separate from hosting/email so your identity can’t be held hostage,
  - use your own domain for email instead of a school-style Gmail address,
  - get a custom phone number if possible,
  - and host your blog on your own domain rather than Medium/Dev.to/freeCodeCamp.
- The post emphasizes **content ownership**:
  - publishing on third-party platforms loses long-term value,
  - and authors should capture photos/videos now for future blog posts.
- Practical career growth advice includes:
  - learn infrastructure basics,
  - watch the suggested “cat” videos,
  - choose boring/stable technology and avoid wasting “innovation tokens” on trendy or risky choices,
  - create silly side projects and launch quickly,
  - iterate based on feedback,
  - and invest in tools like a good microphone and camera if working remotely.
- The author also values:
  - public speaking as a life skill,
  - starting at user groups,
  - using projects and talks as interview assets,
  - and building a reputation by helping others look good.
- The community section encourages:
  - reading about community dynamics,
  - understanding dissenting opinions,
  - and avoiding echo chambers in tech spaces.
