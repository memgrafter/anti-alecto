### TL;DR
Dynomight argues that many socially successful or useful “plans” work best when people do them publicly without explicitly stating the real strategic reason—using examples from marriage, geoengineering, religion, social-status building, meaning-making, and Chinese medicine.

### Key Quote
“Critically, you get everyone to agree that it’s gauche to discuss the reasons for doing all this.”

### Summary
- This essay’s central idea is that some plans depend on **not talking about the plan’s true purpose**.
- It presents **six examples** of this pattern:

  - **Marriage / romance**
    - A couple makes public, costly commitments—wedding, rings, social vows, legal/financial entanglement—to strengthen commitment.
    - The point is not to say, “we are optimizing for commitment,” but to let the ritual and social norms do the work.
    - The behavior becomes stronger over generations because people copy it without thinking about the original strategic logic.

  - **Geoengineering / climate**
    - Spraying sulphuric acid into the atmosphere might cheaply reduce warming, but it has serious risks:
      - worsened air pollution
      - ozone damage
      - droughts
      - sky-color changes
      - no fix for ocean acidification
      - dependence on continual intervention
    - The political concern is that mentioning geoengineering could reduce pressure to cut emissions.
    - So the proposed strategy is: **don’t talk about geoengineering**, at least until emissions are much lower.

  - **Religion / community**
    - Even if one is skeptical of God, religion may provide health and social benefits.
    - Secular substitutes like Ethical Society / Humanist Society / Sunday Assembly can attract odd dynamics:
      - high-commitment people stop attending
      - low-alternative people keep returning
    - A “normal” religion may work better because it sustains a stable community and meaningful shared worldview, even if not everyone believes literally.

  - **Mutual admiration / status networks**
    - If you think your group is right but ignored, you can form a mutual-praise cabal.
    - Early explicit self-promotion backfires, so the group learns to:
      - praise the content itself
      - promote charismatic members and attractive messages
      - treat this as normal, not strategic
    - Over time, outsiders join and copy the behavior.

  - **Meaning / optimism**
    - Choosing to believe that life is meaningful, that things happen for a reason, and that work/family/friends matter can improve well-being.
    - Edgelords who insist on nihilistic views are framed as socially unhelpful.
    - The essay suggests people maintain a better reality by not foregrounding grim truths.

  - **Chinese medicine / Mao**
    - Mao-era China faced a shortage of Western-style doctors and widespread reliance on traditional Chinese medicine.
    - Instead of abrupt replacement, the state pushed “unification” and “critical” acceptance of Chinese medicine.
    - The essay presents this as a pragmatic, non-disruptive way to improve care and avoid backlash, even if some of the system is scientifically weak.
    - It also notes a modern U.S. Senate example: honoring cheap but unproven alternatives.

- The essay’s **main thesis**:
  - Many beneficial systems are sustained by **ritual, norms, ambiguity, and selective silence**.
  - Some things work better when people do them for the “wrong” reasons, or without fully understanding the reasons.
  - This can be read as an argument for **humility** and against the assumption that everything valuable must be openly justified in explicit rational terms.

- The author explicitly says:
  - these kinds of plans are often **good**
  - the point is to examine **why you shouldn’t talk about them**
  - not talking can be:
    - secrecy to avoid interference
    - public action without revealing true motives
    - culturally inherited practices that work precisely because people don’t overanalyze them
