### TL;DR
Hacker News discusses the launch of the Open Source Endowment (OSE), a 501(c)(3) community-run fund for sustainable OSS maintainer grants, with top commenter **edwardloveall** arguing that small grants don’t solve the real problem because maintainers need predictable living-wage income and long-term support.

### Key Quote
“I think my point is that grants may not be the way to get what is desired.”

### Summary
- **Top comment (verbatim):** `"I think my point is that grants may not be the way to get what is desired. Most people need predictable, long term income."`
- **Top commenter:** `edwardloveall`
- **Thread topics:**
  - whether an OSS endowment is a better funding model than ad hoc grants
  - whether OSE’s governance, donor requirements, and “skin in the game” model are healthy or elitist
  - whether 501(c)(3) status is appropriate for funding individual maintainers
  - whether market-based endowments are too risky or too capitalist for OSS support
  - how to select “critical” projects, especially outside GitHub

- **What OSE is:**
  - A **community-driven endowment fund** for funding maintainers of critical open source projects.
  - Donations are invested in a **low-risk portfolio**; only investment income is spent, with the stated goal of about **5%/year** for grants.
  - OSE says it has received **US 501(c)(3)** status.
  - The fund is around **$700K** and has **60+ founding donors**, including founders from **HashiCorp, Elastic, ClickHouse, Supabase, Vue.js, Pydantic, Nginx, Gatsby, n8n, and curl**.
  - The goal is to make funding **open, data-driven, measurable**, and governed by donors/members.

- **OSE’s current and planned grant model:**
  - Initial grants are described as **microgrants around $5K**, though the org says grants can grow as the fund grows.
  - The poster argues that OSS sustainability requires **living-wage-level support**, not one-off grants.
  - They suggest the model may evolve toward **longer-term or tenured maintainer roles**, analogous to academic positions.
  - OSE says it wants to support **critical, high-use, nonprofit/independent OSS**, not commercial product development.

- **Governance and membership:**
  - OSE says **Members** donate **$1,000+/year** and can advise strategy and appoint community-nominated board directors.
  - The poster repeatedly stresses that governance should have **“skin in the game”** and that the org is volunteer-run with minimal operating costs.
  - Some commenters criticize this as **pay-to-play** or insufficiently democratic.

- **Comparison with Open Collective / Open Source Collective:**
  - OSE distinguishes itself from **Open Collective** (payments platform) and **Open Source Collective** (fiscal sponsor).
  - OSE’s pitch is that it is **not just a payments tool or fiscal sponsor**, but a permanent pool of capital whose returns fund grants.
  - The poster says OSE donations may be tax-deductible under 501(c)(3), unlike Open Collective donations.

- **Major debate themes in the replies:**
  - **Grants vs salary-level support:** many commenters agree one-off grants are helpful but not enough for sustained maintenance.
  - **AI and open source:** some worry about LLMs and “vibe-coded” projects flooding the system or undermining OSS; the poster says OSE will focus on “old” critical projects, not AI-generated work.
  - **Governance concerns:** commenters worry about centralized control, VC influence, donor class bias, and elitism in who gets to steer the fund.
  - **Project selection:** users question GitHub-only nominations and ask for broader signals like Debian package usage, downloads, distro installs, and real-world dependency data.
  - **Tax/legal concerns:** several replies debate whether funding individuals or maintainers via 501(c)(3) crosses into impermissible commercial product development. OSE responds that it obtained IRS approval and plans to stay within the scope described in its application.
  - **Historical cautionary tales:** commenters cite **Wikipedia** and **Mozilla** as examples of well-meaning nonprofits losing donor trust through governance drift or spending choices.

- **OSE’s self-description and defense:**
  - The poster frames OSE as a **small, lean, volunteer-run charity startup** rather than a mature institution.
  - They argue that **donor decentralization** is crucial and say they intentionally avoided a model dominated by a few huge funders.
  - They view the endowment as a way to make OSS support more **predictable, scalable, and less dependent on corporate budgets or volatile donations**.
  - They explicitly say the target is **critical public digital infrastructure** and **non-commercial OSS maintenance**.

- **Overall tone of the thread:**
  - Supportive in principle, but heavily scrutinized.
  - Many commenters like the **endowment concept** yet question the **practicality, governance, and legal framing**.
  - The strongest recurring disagreement is whether OSS maintenance should be funded through a **market-invested endowment** at all, versus more direct grants, memberships, or state/public support.
