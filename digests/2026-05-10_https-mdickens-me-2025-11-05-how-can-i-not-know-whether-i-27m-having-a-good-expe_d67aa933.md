### TL;DR
Michael Dickens argues that you can genuinely be unable to tell whether a current experience is “good” because you’re evaluating a different moment than the one you’re having, because experiences are a weighted mix of feelings that aren’t easy to aggregate, and because introspection changes the experience itself.

### Key Quote
“Fundamentally, it is impossible for me to check how I feel while I’m engrossed, because then I wouldn’t be engrossed.”

### Summary
- This is a short philosophical blog post using **Elden Ring boss fights** as the concrete example for a broader question: **why it can be hard to know whether you’re having a good experience while you’re in it**.
- Dickens’s core claim is that “good experiences are good because [they’re] good for me,” but **self-assessment of experience is not straightforward**.
- He gives **three reasons** this is hard:
  - **Wrong moment problem:** when he pauses to introspect, he is no longer in the gameplay moment he wants to evaluate; he is instead experiencing a **loading screen plus frustration**, which may not represent the game experience itself.
  - **Aggregation problem:** he can feel **frustration, excitement, and motivation** at the same time, but figuring out the net result requires **weighing multiple experiences**, which is not an immediate raw sensation.
  - **Observer effect problem:** introspecting **changes the experience**; if he pauses to examine whether he is enjoying himself, he stops being fully immersed, so the act of checking alters what is being checked.
- He adds a personal observation that this confusion is **more common when he is tired**, suggesting that evaluating one’s own experience may depend on **cognitive effort** and mental energy.
- The post ends by summarizing these as the three main explanations for why a person may not know whether their experience is good:
  - the present moment is not the one being introspected,
  - multiple simultaneous experiences must be aggregated,
  - introspection changes the experience.
