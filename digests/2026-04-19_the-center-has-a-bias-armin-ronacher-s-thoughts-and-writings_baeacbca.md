### TL;DR
Armin Ronacher argues that meaningful criticism of AI coding agents requires real hands-on use, so the “center” in new-technology debates is biased toward engagement rather than neutral distance.

### Key Quote
“you have to cross a threshold of use before your criticism becomes grounded in the thing itself rather than in its reputation.”

### Summary
- **Main thesis**
  - Technology debates tend to polarize into outright rejection vs. enthusiastic adoption.
  - Ronacher argues the apparent “middle” is not truly neutral; it is biased toward **engagement**, because informed judgment usually requires direct experience.
  - In practice, the center looks closer to adopters because you must actually use a technology to evaluate it well.

- **Critique from non-users**
  - Criticism from people with little or no direct experience can still be valid:
    - AI coding agents can produce bad code.
    - They can harm skills.
    - Security risks are real.
    - Economics may be unsustainable.
    - Environmental and social consequences are unclear.
    - The hype is exhausting.
  - But such criticism is often **abstract**, relying on secondhand sources, screenshots, anecdotes, conference talks, social media, and company marketing rather than contact with the tool itself.

- **Why the center is not neutral**
  - The “center” is made of people willing to spend time exploring a tool enough to judge it.
  - That requires crossing a **threshold of use**: enough exposure to get past first annoyance and the honeymoon phase.
  - For AI tools, he suggests true understanding may take **weeks**, not hours.
  - This means the middle is populated by people with a bias toward curiosity and experimentation, not indifference.

- **Limits of engagement**
  - Using a tool does **not** automatically make someone wise or unbiased.
  - Enthusiastic adopters may:
    - overvalue novelty,
    - rationalize their investment,
    - overgeneralize from successful use cases,
    - or become overly attached to “progress.”
  - So engagement is necessary for understanding, but it can also distort judgment.

- **Why AI coding agents are especially polarizing**
  - AI is described as a major capability shift with huge amounts of investment attached.
  - Some adopters have bet their future on it, which intensifies the debate.
  - This makes the divide sharper between:
    - people rejecting it without real use,
    - and people embracing it with near-religious enthusiasm.

- **The most grounded criticism often comes from users**
  - Ronacher notes that some of the best criticism comes from people who use coding agents extensively.
  - Example: **Mario** is cited as someone who created a coding agent yet is also highly critical of the space.
  - Such people can speak concretely about:
    - where agents fail,
    - where they waste time,
    - how they can regress code quality,
    - what tooling is needed for them to work better,
    - and which ecosystems they fit poorly.

- **Important nuance**
  - He explicitly rejects the idea that this is a blanket “pro-innovation bias.”
  - Some technologies deserve resistance.
  - Some people who refused a technology early were right.
  - He uses **crypto** as a cautionary example: many projects looked exciting, but the economics eventually failed.

- **Bottom line**
  - The center is not the point equidistant from rejection and enthusiasm.
  - It is the group willing to get close enough to a technology to make a grounded judgment.
  - To criticize a new thing well, you often must use it enough to understand not just that it fails, but **how** and **where** it fails.
