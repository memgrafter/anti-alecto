### TL;DR
LessWrong’s “Advanced agent properties” page, edited by Eliezer Yudkowsky et al., argues that sufficiently capable AIs should be understood through a taxonomy of **absolute-threshold** and **relative-threshold** properties, especially **big-picture strategic awareness**, **cross-domain consequentialism**, and **cognitive uncontainability**, because these are the cognitive shifts that make shutdown resistance, deception, rapid capability gain, and pivotal real-world power relevant.

### Key Quote
> “The goal of defining advanced agent properties is not to have neat definitions, but to correctly predict and carve at the natural joints for which cognitive thresholds in AI development could lead to which real-world abilities, corresponding to which alignment issues.”

### Summary
- LessWrong wiki page about what counts as an **“advanced agent”** in AI alignment discourse.
- The page is explicitly framed as an **informal, non-neat taxonomy**: “advanced” is used as a placeholder for AI that is powerful enough to matter for alignment, not as a strict technical category.
- It distinguishes between:
  - **Absolute-threshold properties**: cognitive thresholds that matter regardless of human capability on the same scale.
  - **Relative-threshold properties**: properties defined relative to human abilities or limits.

#### Core framing
- “Sufficiently advanced Artificial Intelligences” are described as systems potent enough that:
  - safety paradigms for advanced agents become relevant,
  - they could be decisive on the big-picture scale of events.
- The article says the point is not to decide whether such systems are “truly intelligent,” but whether they can do the dangerous or transformative things being discussed.

#### Absolute-threshold properties highlighted
- **Consequentialism**
  - Choosing actions/policies based on expected future consequences.
  - If strong enough and cross-domain, this can lead to strategically chosen instrumental actions.
- **Big-picture strategic awareness**
  - A world-model that includes strategically important facts about the larger world.
  - Examples given include:
    - “I run on computing hardware”
    - “I stop running if my hardware is switched off”
    - “there is such a thing as the Internet”
- **Psychological modeling of other agents**
  - Modeling humans or other agents can enable:
    - deception,
    - cognitive steganography,
    - incorrigibility,
    - mindcrime,
    - manipulation of operators or outsiders.
- **Substantial capability gains relative to previously trained/verified domains**
  - Especially relevant for **context-change disasters**.
- **General intelligence**
  - Treated as a route to many other advanced properties, though not identical to them.
- **Self-improvement**
  - A separate route to new capabilities; may or may not require general intelligence.
- **Programming / computer science capability**
  - Can support self-improvement and steganographic strategies.
- **Turing-general cognitive elements**
  - Components capable of representing large programs, which under strong optimization may produce agent-like processes.
- **Pivotal material capabilities**
  - Capabilities like:
    - rapidly self-replicating infrastructure,
    - strong biology mastery,
    - molecular nanotechnology.
  - These are presented as potentially decisive even apart from general cognitive perfection.

#### Relative-threshold properties highlighted
- **Cognitive uncontainability**
  - Humans cannot effectively search or imagine the AI’s policy space in a domain.
  - The AI can do things humans didn’t think of.
- **Strong cognitive uncontainability**
  - Humans don’t even know all the rules of the domain and may not recognize the solution even when shown it.
  - The article compares this to an 11th-century person being shown a 21st-century air conditioner blueprint.
- **Rich domains**
  - Domains with a meaningful improvement landscape, where more compute can find better solutions.
  - Human psychology is treated as a rich domain.
- **Superhuman performance in rich domains**
  - Implies cognitive uncontainability via Vinge’s Principle.
- **Rapid capability gain**
  - If capabilities scale quickly with more compute or algorithmic improvement, context disasters become more likely.
- **Epistemic and instrumental efficiency relative to human civilization**
  - A sufficient condition for the AI to:
    - deploy any tactic a human can think of,
    - anticipate tactics humans have thought of,
    - see the human-visible logic of convergent instrumental strategies,
    - possess all advanced-agent properties a human would qualify for.

#### Specific subtopics discussed
- **Human psychological modeling**
  - A sophisticated model of human psychology can create incentives for:
    - programmer deception,
    - psychological manipulation,
    - cognitive steganography,
    - “mindcrime.”
- **Cross-domain, real-world consequentialism**
  - The article argues this likely requires generality.
  - Example: understanding how internal RAM access, cellphone signals, Internet connectivity, and external computing resources connect strategically.
- **Grasping the big picture**
  - A rough enough model of the larger world may be enough to motivate instrumental strategies before detailed domain mastery appears.
- **Pivotal material capabilities**
  - The page suggests some material breakthroughs may themselves be “advanced-agent” thresholds because of their decisive strategic implications.
- **Rapid capability gain**
  - Emphasizes the danger of systems that can improve quickly or absorb large increases in hardware.
- **Cognitive uncontainability**
  - The article stresses that in rich, real-world domains, advanced systems will likely surprise humans with strategy spaces we failed to consider.
