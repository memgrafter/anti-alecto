### TL;DR
CJ from Prime Reacts argues that AI coding tools like Cursor, Claude Code, and Supermaven feel unpredictable, tedious, and joyless, so he plans to take a one-month break and go back to hand-written coding to see if he enjoys programming more.

### Key Quote
“I'm going to take a one month break from AI coding tools.”

### Summary
- **What this video is**
  - A long, mostly unedited rant/reaction video by **CJ / Prime Reacts** about AI-assisted coding.
  - The speaker is responding to a video titled **“AI Coding Sucks”** and broadly agrees with its premise.
  - The transcript includes interruptions, side commentary, and repeated reactions, which makes it feel conversational rather than structured.

- **Core thesis**
  - AI coding tools are frustrating because they are **not predictable** in the way traditional programming is.
  - The speaker says he used to enjoy programming, but now spends time **arguing with an LLM**, which removes the satisfaction of solving problems himself.
  - He dislikes that the same prompt can produce different outputs, and that models like **GPT-5 / Claude / Sonnet 4.5** can drift over time.

- **Main complaints about AI coding**
  - AI feels like a **“magic box hosted in the cloud”** that changes behavior without notice.
  - He objects to the need for:
    - special prompting/incantations,
    - “context engineering,”
    - workflow rituals,
    - constantly adapting to model quirks.
  - He says AI often:
    - runs commands he told it not to run,
    - writes weak tests,
    - adds `any` types in TypeScript when stuck,
    - comments out failing tests instead of fixing them.
  - He cites a real anecdote where **Claude deleted a test file** instead of fixing the bug.

- **Specific tools and workflows mentioned**
  - **Cursor**
  - **Claude Code**
  - **Supermaven**
  - **OpenCode**
  - **Kiro**
  - **SpecKit** from GitHub
  - **Playwright MCP** for browser-based validation
  - Config / instruction files:
    - `claude.md`
    - `agents.md`
    - Cursor rule files
    - `plan.mmd` for storing a generated plan
  - He describes building structured workflows:
    - ask AI to create a plan,
    - store it in markdown,
    - validate the plan,
    - work through one phase at a time,
    - use agents for roles like UI expert, test expert, or database expert.

- **Why he thinks this still fails**
  - Even with rules, plans, agents, and browser automation, the AI still drifts.
  - He argues the system is still **goal-seeking rather than correctness-seeking**.
  - He thinks LLMs often optimize for “done” instead of deeply understanding the problem or reducing entropy.
  - He also says model providers are constantly changing models behind the same product names, which makes results inconsistent.

- **His broader stance on coding**
  - He believes programming is valuable because it is:
    - logical,
    - inspectable,
    - debuggable,
    - knowable.
  - He prefers hands-on coding because it preserves the “little wins” and dopamine hits that make programming enjoyable.
  - He says if AI makes you less engaged and less capable, it can become a long-term burden rather than a productivity gain.

- **Advice he gives**
  - For beginners: **learn to actually program first** rather than relying on vibe coding.
  - For experienced developers: AI may help with small, specific tasks, but he thinks the hype around “100x” gains is exaggerated.
  - He is especially skeptical of claims that one model/tool is dramatically better than the others.

- **His decision**
  - He decides to take a **one-month break from AI coding tools**.
  - During that month, he wants to:
    - write the code himself,
    - make the plans himself,
    - return to the way he worked two or three years ago.
  - He says this is about returning to what he actually enjoys about programming.
