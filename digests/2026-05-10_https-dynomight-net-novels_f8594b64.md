### TL;DR
Dynomight argues that novels are valuable less because they’re “good for you” in some abstract sense and more because they offer socially shared context, interiority, authorial vision, and a better use of dead time than phone-scrolling, while also acknowledging that many of the usual pro-novel theories are incomplete or silly.

### Key Quote
> “In the end, I don’t think novels are the best way to spend your time.”

### Summary
- **Article type:** Opinion/essay on why reading novels matters.
- **Main question:** Why should anyone read novels if most of what we remember fades and other activities might be more entertaining or efficient?

- **Opening example:**
  - The author says they barely remember *Crime and Punishment* beyond a vague sketch:
    - Raskolnikov is angsty and commits a random murder.
    - A police inspector keeps hounding him with philosophical rants.
    - The ending is forgotten.
  - The point: even weak memory of a novel may still leave reading feeling worthwhile.

- **Core argument:** The essay walks through multiple possible explanations for why novels matter, without fully committing to any single one.
  - **Theory 1: Status**
    - Reading novels can function as cultural capital.
    - The author thinks this is part of the explanation, but not the whole thing.
  - **Theory 2: Diminishing returns**
    - Reading novels may be valuable because it builds the ability to read novels at all, or because first exposure is especially meaningful.
    - This may explain why adults push kids to read even if they themselves don’t keep reading.
  - **Theory 3: Common language**
    - Shared books create social context and make conversation richer.
    - Examples given: *Catcher in the Rye*, *The Three-Body Problem*, *Infinite Jest*, *Don Quixote*.
    - Novels help people talk about interpretations of characters and themes, unlike more fixed cultural artifacts like sports results.
    - The author thinks books are useful here, but not uniquely so.
  - **Theory 4: Legible mind-space**
    - Novels are especially good at representing characters’ interior thoughts.
    - Other media can do this, but often awkwardly.
    - The author notes that *My Brilliant Friend* works unusually well on TV partly because it uses voice-over heavily.
  - **Theory 5: Purity of vision**
    - Novels are often created by one person, with fewer commercial and collaborative compromises than films.
    - The author claims there are vastly more novels than feature films each year, suggesting novels can preserve highly individual voices.
  - **Theory 6: Some works are just extraordinary**
    - Maybe the real answer is simply that masterpieces like *War and Peace* are obviously worth reading.
    - But most novels people read are not at that level, so this doesn’t explain everything.
  - **Theory 7: Dopamine**
    - Reading is an active, attention-sustaining activity, unlike short-form video consumption.
    - The author contrasts reading with addictive phone/app behavior and suggests phones may be uniquely soul-draining.
  - **Theory 8: Non-fungible time**
    - Novels may not be the best possible use of time, but they can be the best realistic option in many situations:
      - on a train
      - in bed
      - after a long day
    - In those moments, a novel beats doomscrolling.

- **Final stance:**
  - The author’s weakest-but-clearest conclusion is that novels are valuable as a good use of otherwise idle, low-energy time.
  - They’re not necessarily superior to all other activities, and not the best thing in principle, but often better than the alternatives available in real life.
  - They end with a practical joke/extension: apply the same theories to blog posts.
