### TL;DR
Human brain cells grown on a chip were taught to play Doom in about a week, a more complex feat than earlier Pong demos and a step toward biological computers for tasks like controlling robot arms.

### Key Quote
“Doom is vastly more complex than earlier demonstrations, and successfully interacting with it highlights real advances in how living neural systems can be controlled and trained,” says Andrew Adamatzky.

### Summary
- **What happened**
  - New Scientist reports that a clump of human brain cells on a chip learned to play the classic game **Doom**.
  - The performance was **far below human players**, but better than a **randomly firing player**.
  - The work is presented as an advance in **biological computing** rather than as evidence that the cells “think” like a human brain.

- **Background / comparison**
  - In **2021**, **Cortical Labs** demonstrated neuron-powered chips playing **Pong**.
  - That earlier setup used **more than 800,000 living brain cells** grown on **microelectrode arrays** that could send and receive electrical signals.
  - Researchers had to **carefully train** the chips to control the paddles.

- **What was new in this Doom demo**
  - Cortical Labs built an interface that makes these chips easier to program with **Python**.
  - An independent developer, **Sean Cole**, used Python to teach the chips to play Doom in **around a week**.
  - Brett Kagan says this was done in **days** by someone with **relatively little direct biology expertise**, which he presents as evidence of greater accessibility and flexibility.
  - The Doom chip used about **a quarter as many neurons** as the Pong demonstration.

- **Why researchers care**
  - The chip reportedly **learned much faster than traditional silicon-based machine learning systems**.
  - Kagan suggests newer learning algorithms could improve performance further.
  - Researchers emphasize practical possibilities such as:
    - **controlling robot arms**
    - other **real-world biological/hybrid computing** tasks

- **Expert reactions**
  - **Andrew Adamatzky**: says Doom is a major increase in complexity over Pong and shows real progress in controlling and training living neural systems.
  - **Steve Furber**: agrees it is a meaningful step up, but notes major unanswered questions remain, including:
    - how the neurons know what is expected of them
    - how they can “see” the screen without eyes
  - **Yoshikatsu Hayashi**: says the jump in capability is exciting and closer to applications like **robot arm control**; he compares Doom to a simpler version of controlling a whole arm.

- **Caveat / interpretation**
  - Kagan explicitly warns not to over-interpret the result as comparable to a human brain.
  - He frames the biological system as a **material for processing information in special ways** that silicon cannot easily replicate, not as a brain substitute.
