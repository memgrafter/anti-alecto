### TL;DR
Wikipedia overview of **three-dimensional space**: defines 3D as the geometry of points needing three coordinates, then surveys its history, Euclidean/linear-algebraic/calculus formulations, and topological examples like 3-manifolds and the 3-sphere.

### Key Quote
“at least three dimensions are required to tie a knot in a piece of string.”

### Summary
- **Core definition**
  - A three-dimensional space is a mathematical space where **three coordinates** are needed to locate a point.
  - Usually refers to **three-dimensional Euclidean space** `R^3`, which models physical space.
  - More general 3D spaces are called **3-manifolds**.
  - In casual usage, it can also mean a **3D region/solid figure**.

- **Historical development**
  - **Aristotle**: argued that bodies have three dimensions.
  - **Euclid**: Books XI–XIII of *Elements* developed 3D geometry, including perpendicularity, parallelism, polyhedra, and the regular Platonic solids.
  - **Descartes / Fermat / Newton**:
    - Cartesian coordinates made 3D geometry analytic.
    - Fermat’s work helped lay groundwork for calculus.
    - Newton introduced polar coordinates as an alternative.
  - **18th century**:
    - **Clairaut** worked on space curves and curvature.
    - **Euler** developed geodesics, intrinsic coordinates, and curvature results.
    - **Monge** advanced the study of curves and surfaces; together with Euler helped found differential geometry.
  - **19th century**:
    - **Hamilton** used quaternions to represent 3D geometry and introduced “scalar” and “vector.”
    - **Gibbs** and **Wilson** formalized modern dot/cross product notation.
    - **Grassmann**, **Peano**, and **Cayley** contributed abstract vector-space and matrix formulations.

- **Euclidean geometry**
  - **Coordinate systems**
    - Standard 3D Cartesian coordinates use three mutually perpendicular axes: **x, y, z**.
    - Other coordinate systems include **cylindrical** and **spherical** coordinates.
  - **Lines and planes**
    - Two points determine a line.
    - Three points are either collinear or determine a plane.
    - In 3D, lines can be **skew** (non-intersecting and non-coplanar).
    - Planes can intersect in a line, be parallel, or meet in a point/none depending on arrangement.
    - A plane is a **hyperplane** in 3D; lines can be described as intersections of two planes.
    - **Varignon’s theorem**: midpoints of any quadrilateral in `R^3` form a parallelogram and are coplanar.
  - **Spheres and balls**
    - A sphere is the set of points at fixed distance `r` from a center.
    - The enclosed solid is a **ball**.
    - Formulas:
      - Volume of ball: `V = 4/3 π r^3`
      - Surface area of sphere: `A = 4 π r^2`
    - Introduces the **3-sphere** as the boundary of a 4-ball, an example of a **3-manifold**.
  - **Polytopes**
    - There are **nine regular polytopes in 3D**: 5 Platonic solids + 4 Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra.
  - **Surfaces of revolution**
    - Generated by rotating a plane curve about an axis.
    - Line generatrix gives:
      - a **cone** if it intersects the axis
      - a **cylinder** if it is parallel to the axis
  - **Quadric surfaces**
    - General second-degree equation in `x, y, z` defines a quadric surface.
    - Non-degenerate types listed:
      - ellipsoid
      - hyperboloid of one sheet
      - hyperboloid of two sheets
      - elliptic cone
      - elliptic paraboloid
      - hyperbolic paraboloid
    - Notes ruled-surface structure of the one-sheet hyperboloid and hyperbolic paraboloid.

- **Linear algebra**
  - 3D space is characterized by **three independent directions**.
  - A vector in `R^3` is an ordered triple of real numbers.
  - **Dot product**
    - `A · B = A1B1 + A2B2 + A3B3`
    - `||A|| = sqrt(A · A)`
    - Also written `A · B = ||A|| ||B|| cos θ`
    - Example use: computing **work** by a force along a displacement.
  - **Cross product**
    - `A × B` is perpendicular to both vectors.
    - Components are given explicitly, including the Levi-Civita form.
    - Magnitude: `||A × B|| = ||A|| ||B|| |sin θ|`
    - It is not commutative or associative, but satisfies the **Jacobi identity**.
    - `(\mathbb R^3, ×)` is isomorphic to the Lie algebra `so(3)`.
    - The binary vector cross product with vector output exists only in **3 and 7 dimensions**.
  - **Abstract description**
    - Physical 3D space can be modeled as a **3D real vector space** `V`, abstractly isomorphic to `R^3`.
    - `R^3` has a **preferred standard basis** because it is a Cartesian product; a general vector space does not.
    - This distinction matters in rotationally symmetric problems, where no basis should be preferred.
  - **Affine description**
    - Physical space can also be seen as a **3D affine space** `E(3)`.
    - This “forgets the origin,” making **translation invariance** explicit.
  - **Inner product space**
    - The dot product is an inner product giving notions of **length, angle, and orthogonality**.
    - Different bases related by rotations can realize the same inner product structure.

- **Calculus**
  - Vector calculus in `R^3` studies differential and integral operations on fields.
  - **Gradient**
    - Points in direction of greatest increase of a scalar field.
  - **Divergence**
    - Measures net outflow/source-sink behavior of a vector field.
  - **Curl**
    - Measures local rotation/circulation of a vector field.
  - **Line integrals**
    - Scalar line integral accumulates a function along a curve.
    - Vector line integral computes work/circulation along a path.
  - **Surface integrals**
    - Integrate over a parameterized surface using the cross product of partial derivatives as the area element.
  - **Volume integrals**
    - Triple integrals over 3D regions.
  - **Fundamental theorem of line integrals**
    - A line integral of a gradient field depends only on endpoints.
  - **Stokes’ theorem**
    - Relates surface integral of curl to boundary line integral.
  - **Divergence theorem**
    - Relates volume integral of divergence to flux through the boundary surface.

- **Topology**
  - 3D has special topological behavior, including the fact that **knots require at least three dimensions**.
  - Generic 3D spaces are **3-manifolds**, locally like `R^3` but globally potentially curved or otherwise nontrivial.
  - The article links this to curved spacetime in general relativity.

- **Finite geometry**
  - Uses projective spaces like **PG(3,2)** to test dimension concepts.
  - More generally, for any finite field `GF(q)`, there is a projective 3-space `PG(3,q)`.
  - Mentions skew lines and reguli in that setting.
