### TL;DR
Dynomight explains large language models as next-word predictors, focusing on why recent LLMs got good: huge compute, huge datasets, modern Transformers, and especially fine-tuning plus prompt engineering for chat-style behavior.

### Key Quote
“People are sometimes dumb and sometimes smart, sometimes mean and sometimes nice. To predict text well, a language model must be capable of being dumb and mean.”

### Summary
- **What an LLM is**
  - A large language model is a function trained to predict the next token/word from a window of prior text.
  - Text is generated by repeatedly predicting likely next tokens and sampling from those predictions.
  - The post frames this as the minimal background needed for understanding scaling laws and LLM progress.

- **Why LLMs suddenly got much better**
  - **More compute**
    - Modern GPUs are vastly more powerful than old hardware.
    - A single NVIDIA V100 would take roughly **355 years** to train GPT-3.
    - PaLM used **several thousand TPUv4 chips** for months; total compute was about **8x GPT-3**.
  - **More data**
    - GPT-3 was trained on about **300 billion tokens**.
    - That’s described as around **270,000 Shakespeares**.
    - Chinchilla was trained on about **1.3 million Shakespeares**.
  - **Better techniques**
    - Autodiff, optimization heuristics, improved ML ops, better function choices for text, and especially the **Transformer** architecture (introduced in 2017).
  - **Better hardware/clustering**
    - Modern training uses clusters of thousands of GPUs/TPUs working together.

- **Examples of model capability**
  - The post gives BLOOM a passive-aggressive prompt and shows multiple generated continuations.
  - The examples are used to illustrate that LLMs can produce fluent, context-sensitive text, even if the content is odd.

- **What training data comes from**
  - Training data is measured in **tokens**, not words.
  - A token is usually a word fragment, punctuation, or part of a word.
  - Rough rule of thumb: **1 token ≈ 4 characters or ¾ of a word**.
  - GPT-3’s sources mentioned:
    - **Common Crawl** — 410B tokens
    - **WebText2** — 19B tokens, pages linked from Reddit posts with at least 3 upvotes from 2005–2020
    - **Books1** — 12B tokens, poorly described
    - **Books2** — 55B tokens, likely books corpus but not clearly specified
    - **Wikipedia** — 3B tokens
  - Important point: data sources are **weighted differently** and heavily **filtered** because raw web text performs poorly.

- **Secrecy around datasets**
  - The post criticizes how little is publicly disclosed about data curation.
  - It notes that papers often cite prior papers in a chain until the source becomes essentially “proprietary.”
  - GPT-3 and PaLM are described as being vague about collection procedures, while newer projects like **The Pile** and **LLaMA** are somewhat more transparent.

- **How LLMs are used**
  - Two main ways:
    - **Fine-tuning**
      - Train first on broad text, then on narrower question-answer or task-specific data.
      - Sometimes includes human ratings of output quality.
    - **Prompt engineering**
      - Carefully designed prompts can strongly steer model behavior.
  - The post argues these two methods are a huge part of why chat systems like **ChatGPT** and **Bing Chat** feel much better than generic language models.

- **Why prompt engineering works**
  - The author’s explanation: models must be capable of many “modes” of language, including dumb, mean, or weird modes, because those patterns exist in training data.
  - A prompt can push generation into the desired mode.
  - Rapid experimentation with prompts has led to quick progress.

- **Overall stance**
  - The post is a clear, accessible explainer rather than a deeply technical paper.
  - It is mildly skeptical of secrecy in data practices, but broadly optimistic/curious about LLM capabilities.
  - It ends by saying the future usefulness and limits of LLMs are still unclear, and points to a next post for further discussion.
