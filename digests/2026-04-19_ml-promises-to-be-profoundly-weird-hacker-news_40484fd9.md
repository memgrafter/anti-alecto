Hacker News thread on Kyle Kingsbury’s essay arguing that ML/LLMs are “profoundly weird” and often behave like plausible-sounding “bullshit machines,” with the top comment from `u/bensyverson` pushing back that the models are improving and many failures reflect human misuse more than a fundamental flaw.

### Summary
- **Content type:** Social thread on HN discussing a linked essay by Kyle Kingsbury (`aphyr.com/posts/411-the-future-of-everything-is-lies-i-guess`).
- **Thread scale:** 615 points, 180 comments captured total, but only 10 top-level comments were captured in the provided scrape sample.
- **Top comment (verbatim):** "I get the frustration, but it's reductive to just call LLMs "bullshit machines" as if the models are not improving. The current flagship models are not perfect, but if you use GPT-2 for a few minutes, it's incredible how much the industry has progressed in seven years.It's true that people don't have a good intuitive sense of what the models are good or bad at (see: counting the Rs in "strawberry"), but this is more a human limitation than a fundamental problem with the technology."
- **Top commenter:** `u/bensyverson`
- **Thread topics:**
  - Whether LLMs are best understood as “bullshit machines” / confabulation engines
  - Whether model failures are fundamental architecture limits or mostly human misuse / poor expectations
  - The significance of improvement over time, especially compared with GPT-2-era models
  - The scale problem: errors that humans also make, but faster and at much larger volume
  - Real-world safety, trust, and whether “good enough” depends on the use case

- **Main claim of the linked essay as reflected in the thread:**
  - LLMs produce plausible-sounding output without a truth-anchoring mechanism.
  - They can be very useful, but their confidence and fluency can disguise factual or reasoning errors.
  - The author frames this as a broader warning that ML systems can be strange, brittle, and socially risky.
- **Key reply clusters:**
  - **Pro-improvement / pro-utility:** Several commenters say the models have improved dramatically and are already useful for coding and other constrained tasks.
  - **“Bullshit” is the right term:** Others argue the term is academically grounded and accurately captures probability-driven text generation without truth guarantees.
  - **Human analogy debate:** A recurring subthread compares LLM confabulation to human self-deception, with disagreement over whether that analogy is meaningful.
  - **Scale and trust concerns:** Some commenters stress that even low error rates become unacceptable when scaled to millions of users or used in high-stakes settings.
  - **Hype / commercialization criticism:** Multiple replies criticize AI marketing, suggesting the industry sells capabilities beyond what the systems reliably deliver.
- **Concrete examples mentioned in the discussion:**
  - GPT-2 as a benchmark for how far models have progressed
  - “counting the Rs in ‘strawberry’” as a toy example of model weakness
  - LLMs making up facts about sports trivia, recipes, board game rules, and coding bugs
  - Concerns about using LLMs for misinformation, deception, and high-stakes decision-making
