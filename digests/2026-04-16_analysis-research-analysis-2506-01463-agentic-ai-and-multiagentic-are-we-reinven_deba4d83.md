### TL;DR
This position paper argues that “Agentic AI” and “Multiagentic AI” mostly rebrand older intelligent-agent and multi-agent system ideas, and it proposes grounding modern LLM agents in decades of classical MAS engineering, coordination, and trust mechanisms.

### Key Quote
“the current hype cycle is effectively marketing decades-old concepts—"intelligent agents" and "multi-agent systems" (MAS)—as novel inventions.”

### Summary
- **Document type:** Position paper / conceptual analysis by **V. Botti**
- **Method:** A **critical literature review and genealogy tracing** of the term “agentic,” connecting it back to social sciences, philosophy, and classical AI
- **Central thesis:**
  - The current “Agentic AI” / “Multiagentic AI” discourse is largely **terminological redundancy**
  - It repackages established work on **intelligent agents** and **multi-agent systems (MAS)** as if it were new
  - This creates a risk of **“reinventing the wheel”** and ignoring proven engineering foundations

- **Main critique of current LLM-agent hype:**
  - Modern agent frameworks often neglect classical agent properties:
    - **Autonomy**
    - **Reactivity**
    - **Proactivity**
    - **Social capability**
  - They tend to rely on **ad hoc implementations** rather than established standards
  - The paper warns that the field may fragment into incompatible agent platforms if it does not converge on shared engineering principles

- **Historical / conceptual point:**
  - The paper argues that the genealogy of “agentic” thinking predates current generative AI trends
  - It emphasizes that foundational research already developed:
    - agent architectures
    - communication languages
    - coordination algorithms
    - agreement technologies

- **Proposed direction:**
  - Retrofit LLM-based agents with classical MAS mechanisms, including:
    - **Structured communication protocols**
      - Self-Referential Communication
      - Agora
    - **Coordination mechanisms**
      - task assignment
      - consensus building
      - voting protocols
    - **Agreement technologies**
      - automated negotiation
      - argumentation frameworks
      - trust models

- **Comparative framing:**
  - Classical MAS are presented as having clear categories such as:
    - reactive agents
    - deliberative agents
    - goal-based and utility-based agents
    - learning agents
  - Modern LLM-based agents are contrasted as powerful but often less rigorous in design

- **Contributions claimed by the paper:**
  1. Clarifies terminology and pushes back against buzzword-heavy framing
  2. Connects modern LLM agents to older MAS theory and engineering
  3. Advocates for scientific rigor and reuse of established standards

- **Results / limits:**
  - No experiments, benchmarks, or quantitative evaluation
  - Findings are **qualitative and conceptual**
  - Main conclusion is that current LLM-agent systems often lack the structural robustness of classical MAS
