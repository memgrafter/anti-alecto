### TL;DR
A long Lobsters comment thread about “AI tribalism” argues over whether criticism of LLMs is principled or motivated, with recurring disputes about copyright, energy use, workers’ rights, education, assistive use, and whether coding agents are actually useful.

### Key Quote
“AI is threatening - it hits right at the heart of our value as knowledge workers and software engineers.”

### Summary
- The thread centers on a post reacting to pro-AI writing that dismisses anti-AI concerns as tribal or motivated reasoning.
- Main fault line: whether people opposing LLMs are
  - raising real ethical/social concerns, or
  - selectively invoking concerns like copyright, energy, and labor only because AI threatens their status or jobs.
- Repeated issues debated:
  - **Copyright**: whether model training is fair use, whether corporate scraping has always been tolerated, and whether current LLM companies are being treated differently because of scale and visibility.
  - **Energy / water usage**: some commenters argue these concerns were present before AI; others say AI changes the scale and political urgency.
  - **Workers’ rights**: several comments frame AI as increasing leverage for employers and worsening conditions for junior devs and knowledge workers.
  - **Education**: LLMs are described as disrupting written assessment, encouraging shortcutting, but also as a 24/7 tutor for learners.
  - **Accessibility**: a blind programmer describes LLMs as assistive tech that can reduce labor and improve access, complicating blanket anti-AI positions.
- Many participants object to the tone of “AI paternalism”:
  - calling critics irrational, scared, or dishonest is seen as disrespectful and overly tribal.
  - others counter that pro-AI posts often ignore or minimize the baseline harms.
- There is a long subthread on whether LLM use is “inevitable”:
  - one side says the capability is now broadly known, too cheap, and too widely distributed to stop.
  - the other side says social rejection or regulation could still meaningfully limit adoption.
- Practical coding-agent discussion:
  - Some users say agents like Claude Code can help with specific tasks and small projects.
  - Others report little productivity gain in large codebases, where the main work is understanding invariants, architecture, and requirements rather than typing code.
  - One commenter gives concrete examples of agent-built projects and transcripts.
  - Another thread discusses Cursor’s FastRender browser project and disputes marketing claims about it being “pulled in Servo.”
- Several comments argue the real issue is not just utility but **power and control**:
  - unaccountable companies controlling the chain from source material to generated output,
  - the risk of replacing human labor,
  - and the political consequences of mass adoption.
- The conversation is highly adversarial but also self-correcting in places:
  - multiple commenters soften their claims,
  - clarify absolute language like “nobody,”
  - and distinguish between sincere ethical concern and opportunistic rhetoric.
- A recurring personal theme is discomfort:
  - some users say they use LLMs despite misgivings, comparing it to tolerating the “sausage-making” of consumer capitalism.
  - others reject that framing as shallow ethics or as an excuse for motivated reasoning.
