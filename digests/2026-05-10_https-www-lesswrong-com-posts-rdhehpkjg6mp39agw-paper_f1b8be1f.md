### TL;DR
dynomight’s LessWrong post argues that paper is underrated not as a storage medium but as a tool for thinking, and in the top comment Radford Neal asks whether whiteboards plus photos now dominate that role.

### Key Quote
“Optimize for thinking.”

### Summary
- **Post type:** short opinion/essay about note-taking and personal workflow
- **Main thesis:**
  - Paper’s real value is helping you think: scribbling, drawing, and arranging ideas physically can make cognition easier.
  - Paper is bad for long-term storage because it is hard to search, cross-reference, copy, and modify.
  - Therefore, people should not optimize paper systems for archival usefulness; they should optimize for the thinking experience.

- **Core observations about paper:**
  - **Paper works best for active thinking**, not as a repository.
  - **A paper workflow needs a system** or it becomes too frictionful to use.
    - Example system can be very simple, like “write on a notepad and throw pages out weekly.”
  - **User experience matters**:
    - Better pens and paper can make writing feel better and improve thinking.
    - The author dislikes lined paper and points out physical annoyances like bad notebook bindings and crushed spirals.
  - **Categorization is difficult**:
    - Separate notebooks for different topics often fail because real life creates edge cases.
    - Managing multiple notebooks becomes a hassle.

- **Author’s paper systems over time:**
  - College system: steal paper from printers, scribble on it, leave loose stacks around.
  - Notebook phase: tried many notebooks, but hated crossed-out “trash” and the inability to comfortably keep visible stacks of pages.
  - Notecard phase:
    - More flexible for trying ideas, moving cards around, and discarding mistakes.
    - But too small for substantial work.
    - Stacks of cards accumulated and became cumbersome.
  - **Current preferred system:**
    - Use **three-hole punched printer paper**
    - Write on it
    - Keep everything in **one three-ring notebook in chronological order**
    - When full, remove pages, add brown cardstock covers, fasten with brass fasteners, and shelve it permanently

- **Practical stance:**
  - Paper is for “making the neurons in your brain fire good,” not for retrieval later.
  - The author explicitly frames paper and notes software as **complements**:
    - Notes software is better for searchable/reusable notes
    - Paper is better for fast, flexible, satisfying thinking
  - The post itself was drafted on paper and then retyped/edited in Obsidian.
