### TL;DR
Hacker News discussion of “We’ve freed Cookie’s Bustle from copyright hell” centers on whether the museum could use 17 U.S.C. § 512(f) to fight allegedly bad-faith DMCA takedowns, with commenters debating Harigai/Brandon White’s motives, the role of RODIK/Graceware, and the asymmetry of forcing a museum to spend far more than the takedown filer.

### Summary
- **Top comment (verbatim):** “Detailed story, very helpful.  
  You nerd baited me, so I went ahead and read 17 U.S.C. § 512(f).”
- **Top commenter:** not explicitly named in the scrape
- **Thread topics:**
  - Whether **17 U.S.C. § 512(f)** gives the museum a practical path to sue over allegedly false copyright claims
  - How **DMCA takedowns** can be abused, especially when automated or filed in bad faith
  - Whether the filer is **Harigai**, **Brandon White**, the original developer, or someone connected to **RODIK/Graceware**
  - The cost imbalance described as **“asymmetric copyright warfare”**
  - Whether platforms should do more **Fair Use review** before removing content

- One commenter quotes the statute directly: if someone “knowingly materially misrepresents” that material is infringing, they can be liable for damages, costs, and attorneys’ fees under **17 U.S.C. § 512(f)**.
- Several replies argue that:
  - The museum could potentially sue in a favorable U.S. venue such as **Northern California, SDNY, or Delaware**.
  - The strongest deterrent would be one successful case against the takedown provider and the person behind the notices.
  - The hard part is proving the filer **knew** the claims were false, especially if they insist they genuinely believe they own the IP.
- The thread repeatedly returns to the idea that the takedown campaign is economically lopsided:
  - The takedown filer reportedly spent **a few thousand dollars**.
  - The museum likely incurred **$200k+** in time and legal fees.
  - This imbalance is explicitly described as **“asymmetric copyright warfare.”**
- There is significant speculation about who is behind the claims:
  - Some suspect **Harigai** because the notices and surrounding behavior fit a person with access to the original game materials.
  - Others argue the evidence could also fit someone who found or inherited old assets and is trying to create a retroactive copyright claim.
  - One commenter compares the situation to **Craig Wright**-style identity/ownership claims.
- The thread discusses the possibility that the filer had access to non-public materials:
  - **High-res 3D renders**
  - **Design documents**
  - **Source code commented in Japanese**
  - Commenters note that possessing such material does not necessarily prove ownership of the IP.
- A recurring counterpoint is that **ownership is messy** and copyright disputes often require courts, not platforms, to decide:
  - Some users say platforms are justified in defaulting to takedowns because copyright is hard to verify privately.
  - Others argue platforms go beyond what the law requires and should do at least basic **Fair Use** review.
- The discussion also notes a key practical detail about DMCA procedure:
  - A recipient can **counter-notice** and assert fair use or ownership.
  - If the claimant does not sue, the platform may restore the content.
- The broader policy argument in the thread is that large platforms like YouTube should not outsource adjudication entirely to claimants:
  - Some commenters say automated systems and content-ID style workflows are too easy to abuse.
  - Others say requiring platforms to evaluate every dispute themselves is unrealistic and expensive.
- A few replies broaden the point beyond this specific case:
  - Copyright terms and ownership are described as confusing across jurisdictions.
  - Some argue copyright should require clearer registration and expiration rules.
  - Others insist the current system already creates enough uncertainty that platforms must be cautious.
