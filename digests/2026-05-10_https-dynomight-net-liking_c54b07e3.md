### TL;DR
Dynomight argues that “liking” is partly a trainable mental habit: by changing the stories you tell yourself, you can sometimes come to enjoy things you thought you disliked, but some preferences are deeper, some are self-misconceptions, and some things remain genuinely hard to change.

### Key Quote
“Lesson: You can have an incorrect concept of self.”

### Summary
- **Article type:** reflective essay / life-philosophy piece about taste, preference, and self-perception.
- **Core thesis:** dislike is not always fixed; sometimes it’s caused by identity, memory, social context, or old associations rather than the thing itself.
- The author proposes a kind of hobby: **take something you don’t like and try to like it** as a way to probe human nature.

#### Main examples and lessons
- **Michael Jackson in Paris**
  - The author noticed French people playing Michael Jackson and eventually decided, “they’re right.”
  - This is used to show that social context and repeated exposure can shift taste.

- **Cooked spinach**
  - Childhood coercion around eating spinach made the food feel tied to autonomy and parental control, not just flavor.
  - As an adult, the author found that changing the mental frame helped: imagining spinach as an exotic dish served by monks in Japan made it feel more appealing.
  - **Lesson:** sometimes we dislike things because we’ve built an identity around disliking them.

- **Country music**
  - The author can appreciate some country songs, but still can’t genuinely like the genre.
  - They note peer-group norms and their desire to be a “cultural omnivore,” but conclude the problem may be deeper: they only want to want to like country music.
  - **Lesson:** some subconscious layers are harder to rewrite than others.

- **Raisins / Oasis**
  - A friend pointed out that the author says they “like raisins” but never actually wants raisins.
  - Likewise, the author may think they love Oasis, but when they listen, it isn’t that good.
  - **Lesson:** people can have an inaccurate concept of their own preferences.

- **Television**
  - The author is unusually picky about TV, believing it can be high art but often finding most shows to be “slop.”
  - This is presented as a different kind of problem: unlike music, refusing most TV is frustrating because it leaves too little acceptable content.
  - Possible explanations offered:
    - most TV is actually bad,
    - lots of it is fine but doesn’t appeal to the author,
    - or it’s hard to frame TV in a way that makes it enjoyable.
  - **Lesson:** some preferences are simply hard to change.

- **Air travel**
  - The captain’s phrase “sit back and enjoy the ride” is treated skeptically, since flying often feels like something to endure.
  - Still, the author argues this points to an important idea: people can experience the same situation very differently.
  - Some people probably do enjoy flights, which suggests that, in principle, the author might too.
  - **Lesson:** even when you can’t control the external situation, you may have power over your experience of it.

#### Overall takeaway
- The essay blends humor and self-observation to argue that taste is layered:
  - some dislikes are socially inherited,
  - some are tied to identity and memory,
  - some are inaccurate self-stories,
  - and some are stubbornly real.
- The broader message is optimistic but limited: **it’s worth trying to like things, because perception can change—but not everything is equally malleable.**
