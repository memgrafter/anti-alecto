### TL;DR
`@raw_works` argues that Recursive Language Models (RLMs) are the next reasoning paradigm because they fuse reasoning and tool use into one recursive inference loop, and the post claims recent benchmarks show big gains on long-context, memory, and long-reasoning tasks while also noting cost, unpredictability, and recursion-specific failure modes.

### Key Quote
"RLMs collapse reasoning and tool use into a single inference abstraction: the model treats its own prompt as an environment it can inspect, slice, and recursively query."

  - Recursive Language Models (RLMs) as a new inference abstraction

- The post’s central thesis is that **RLMs unify reasoning and tool use** by making the model treat its prompt as an environment it can inspect, partition, and recursively query.
- It defines a Recursive Language Model as an inference setup where:
  - the prompt is exposed as a manipulable variable in a REPL-like loop
  - the model can slice the prompt into regions
  - it can make recursive subcalls over those slices
  - recursion ends at the base model’s ordinary forward pass
- The author claims this matters because **context size is no longer a hard ceiling**: the paper reports RLMs handling inputs up to **two orders of magnitude beyond the base model’s context window** and beating vanilla frontier LLMs plus common long-context scaffolds on **four long-context tasks**.

- The post gives a history of how the field evolved:
  - **2022:** chain-of-thought and self-consistency show reasoning gains from spending more inference compute without tools.
  - **Late 2022:** ReAct combines reasoning traces with external actions, making tool use part of the reasoning loop.
  - **2023:** Toolformer and function-calling APIs make tool use more structured and reliable.
  - **2023:** Tree of Thoughts shows internal search over reasoning paths without requiring tools.
  - **2024:** OpenAI o1 frames “thinking more before responding” as a product category, initially separate from function calling.
  - **2024:** Anthropic’s Claude tool use and computer use push toward agentic interaction with real interfaces.
  - **Late 2024–2025:** Gemini messaging emphasizes native tool use and a separate thinking capability.
- The author’s point is that RLMs are where this split between “thinking” and “acting” starts to collapse into one abstraction.

- The empirical story is organized as a progression through three failure modes:
  - **long context**
  - **memory**
  - **long reasoning**
- The post says benchmark results across these areas increasingly support RLMs as more than a prompt hack.

- On benchmarks and results:
  - **Oolong** is presented as a hard long-context reasoning benchmark; the author says many frontier models score under 50% on its splits at 128K context.
  - **LongMemEval** is described as a long-term interactive memory benchmark with **500 questions** spanning extraction, multi-session reasoning, temporal reasoning, knowledge updates, and abstention.
  - The original public RLM write-up reportedly showed:
    - a **GPT-5-mini RLM** beating **GPT-5** by **more than 2×** on an Oolong split
    - better performance than **ReAct + test-time indexing/retrieval** on a BrowseComp-Plus-derived task
    - no visible degradation even at **10M+ input tokens**
  - The formal arXiv paper is said to report:
    - inputs up to **100× beyond** context windows
    - better results than vanilla frontier LLMs and common long-context scaffolds on **four tasks**
    - a fine-tuned **RLM-Qwen3-8B** improving **28.3% on average** over its base model
  - The author’s own experiments are cited:
    - **Gemini 3 Flash** baseline on LongMemEval: **87.2%**
    - with tools + delegation prompt: **89.2%**
    - with an observational-memory-style scaffold: **89.8%**
    - earlier tool-use REPL path: **77.6%** on LongMemEval
  - Follow-up work is described as mixed:
    - one reproduction finds **depth-1 recursion helps** but deeper recursion can “overthink,” increasing cost and hurting accuracy
    - another argues **uncertainty-aware self-reflective program search** can beat RLM by up to **22%** under the same time budget
    - another claims off-the-shelf coding agents outperform published SOTA by **17.3% on average**
  - The strongest “reasoning” evidence comes from **LongCoT**, a benchmark of **2,500 expert-designed long-horizon chain-of-thought problems**:
    - best published models at release are still under **10%**
    - the author claims **Claude Sonnet 4.5 + DSPy.RLM** reaches **45.4%** vs **2.6%** without recursion/tools on LongCoT-Mini
    - small models like **Qwen3-8B** jump from **0/507** to **33/507 (6.5%)**
    - **Qwen3.5-9B + DSPy.RLM** reaches **15.69%** on full LongCoT
    - **Qwen3.5-27B + DSPy.RLM** reaches **22.18%**

- The author’s interpretation is that these results suggest recursive, tool-mediated processing is becoming the best way to handle problems where a single forward pass is too shallow for:
  - navigating large structures
  - decomposing entangled context
  - aggregating many local observations into a global answer

- The post is also explicit about limitations:
  - **cost** is high
  - **latency** can be long
  - naive recursion creates **unpredictable, unbounded runtime**
  - models often **do not naturally “act recursively”** even if recursion is conceptually in their training data
  - good recursion likely needs **post-training in an RLM harness**
  - the “reward function for optimal recursion” is posed as an open, potentially huge problem

- The author also frames a possible strategic consequence:
  - recursive scaffolds may let **smaller, cheaper models** achieve capabilities previously associated with much larger frontier models
  - that could shift power away from the biggest GPU-rich labs, at least directionally

- The post ends with a short list of ways to start building with RLMs, including:
  - the reference implementation from the RLM paper authors
  - a **DSPy integration**
  - a **TypeScript DSPy-style framework**
  - the author’s own CLI wrapper for directory-as-context workflows
  - a recursive coding agent using `rlm_query`, `rlm_map`, and isolated workspaces
- It closes with a light joke about **fractals**, reinforcing the recursive theme.
