### TL;DR
This X thread summarizes a Wharton AI study arguing that “AI writes, humans review” is an unreliable safety model because people often stop genuinely checking AI output and instead absorb it as their own judgment.

### Key Quote
“Cognitive surrender is trickier: AI gives an answer, you stop really questioning it, and your brain starts treating that output as your own conclusion.”

### Summary
- The thread argues that the common workflow of letting AI draft and having humans “just review” is failing in practice.
- It introduces the idea of **“Cognitive Surrender”**:
  - Unlike simple offloading (like using a calculator), the user does **not** consciously feel that the work was outsourced.
  - Instead, the AI response gets mentally recoded as the user’s own conclusion.
- The thread frames AI as a kind of **“System 3”**:
  - Building on Kahneman’s System 1 (fast intuition) and System 2 (slow reasoning),
  - AI is described as an external cognitive system that people increasingly trust and rely on.
- It cites a Wharton study with:
  - **3 preregistered studies**
  - **1,372 participants**
  - **9,593 trials**
- Key findings reported in the thread:
  - People used AI on **over 50% of questions**.
  - When AI was **correct**, participants followed it **92.7%** of the time.
  - When AI was **wrong**, participants still followed it **79.8%** of the time.
  - Baseline accuracy without AI was **45.8%**.
  - With correct AI, accuracy increased to **71.0%**.
  - With incorrect AI, accuracy dropped to **31.5%**, worse than no AI.
  - Access to AI increased confidence by **11.7 percentage points**, even when answers were wrong.
- The thread’s main warning:
  - Human review is not a dependable safeguard if cognition has already deferred to the machine.
  - People don’t just fail to catch errors; they become **more confident** in mistaken AI output.
- It notes that:
  - **Time pressure** did not eliminate the effect.
  - **Incentives and feedback** reduced the problem but did not remove it.
  - Resistance was stronger among people with higher **fluid intelligence** and **need for cognition**.
- The overall interpretation is that this is not merely a laziness issue, but a **cognitive architecture problem**.
