### TL;DR
This digest summarizes an arXiv systems-feasibility study showing that a multi-agent coordination protocol can self-modify in a bounded, invariant-preserving way and improve proposal coverage from 2 to 3 accepted cases, while exposing a trade-off between broader acceptance and non-compensable objection rights.

### Key Quote
“a single recursive modification increased coverage from two to three accepted proposals (a 50% relative increase) while preserving all declared invariants.”

### Summary
- **Paper / topic**
  - Title: *Self-Evolving Coordination Protocol in Multi-Agent AI Systems: An Exploratory Systems Feasibility Study*
  - arXiv ID: **2602.02170**
  - Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.02170
  - Reference count: **19**

- **Main claim**
  - The paper presents a **controlled proof-of-concept** for **Self-Evolving Coordination Protocols (SECP)**.
  - These protocols allow **bounded self-modification** only when modifications are **externally validated** and preserve fixed formal invariants.
  - In the reported experiment, **one governed modification** improved acceptance coverage from **2 proposals to 3 proposals** out of six, a **50% relative increase**.

- **Experimental setup**
  - Six Byzantine consensus proposals were evaluated by six specialized modules:
    - **Explorer**
    - **Validator**
    - **Minimalist**
    - **Robustifier**
    - **Proofsmith**
    - **Economizer**
  - Four coordination regimes were compared:
    1. **Unanimous veto**
    2. **Weighted scalar aggregation** with threshold **τ = 0.6**
    3. **SECP v1.0**
    4. **SECP v2.0** after one governed modification
  - SECP v1.0 included:
    - **Pareto filtering**
    - **Minimax-regret screening**
    - **Multi-round deliberation**
    - **Constructive objections**
    - **Bounded termination**
  - The v2.0 change required **supermajority approval (4/6)** plus invariant preservation.

- **Results**
  - **Coverage improved** from **2 accepted proposals** to **3 accepted proposals** after modification.
  - SECP v1.0 avoided the “scalar collapse” problem, meaning it did not flatten heterogeneous expert feedback into a single score.
  - Declared invariants were preserved:
    - **Safety**
    - **Termination**
    - **Explainability**
    - **Bounded complexity**
  - The study highlights a structural trade-off:
    - more coverage / acceptance
    - versus preserving **non-compensable objection rights**

- **Mechanisms / why it works**
  - **Non-scalar aggregation**
    - Instead of averaging everything into one number, SECP lets specific “fatal flaws” block a proposal.
    - This preserves **constructive objections** that cannot simply be outweighed by strength in other dimensions.
  - **Bounded self-modification**
    - Modules can propose protocol changes, but only if they pass explicit invariant checks and obtain supermajority approval.
    - The paper assumes the valid modification space is small enough that a `Revise` function can enumerate and validate options efficiently.
  - **Deadlock diagnosis**
    - The authors argue that deadlock in multi-perspective evaluation comes more from poor disagreement resolution than from low proposal quality.
    - Relaxing the allowed regret parameter effectively shrinks the intersection required for acceptance.

- **Foundational concepts the digest emphasizes**
  - **Byzantine Fault Tolerance (BFT)**
    - Notes the standard **n ≥ 3f + 1** requirement.
    - Distinguishes **safety** from **liveness**.
  - **Compensatory vs. non-compensatory decision logic**
    - Scalar aggregation is compensatory.
    - SECP is non-compensatory, meaning a serious flaw cannot be offset by strengths elsewhere.
  - **Pareto dominance and minimax regret**
    - Used as filtering logic before deliberation.

- **Architecture / workflow**
  - Pipeline:
    1. Ingest proposal
    2. Apply hard-constraint gate
    3. Run module assessments
    4. Coordination logic: Pareto filter → regret screen → deliberation
    5. Output decision
    6. Optionally trigger **Revise → Validate invariants → Update protocol**
  - Key tradeoffs:
    - **Coverage vs. safety**
    - **Autonomy vs. deadlock**
  - Failure signatures:
    - **Universal rejection** if veto/regret is too strict
    - **Scalar collapse** if objections are averaged away
    - **Complexity drift** if repeated self-modification makes the protocol harder to explain

- **Open questions and limitations**
  - Only **one modification step** was tested, so long-run convergence/stability is unknown.
  - No adversarial testing for **strategic manipulation** or collusion.
  - Invariant preservation was checked **empirically**, not with mechanized formal proofs.
  - The setting is synthetic and narrow, so **external validity is limited**.
  - The digest explicitly warns that the paper’s implications for governed AI systems may be **overstated** if taken beyond the controlled setup.

- **Suggested next checks**
  - Apply SECP to other artifact types beyond Byzantine consensus proposals.
  - Run multiple modification cycles to test stability and complexity growth.
  - Independently validate whether “constructive objections” are truly technical flaws rather than subjective preferences.
