From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

A [chemical reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction") is **autocatalytic** if one of the reaction [products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_(chemistry) "Product (chemistry)") is also a [catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") for the same reaction.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-1) Many forms of autocatalysis are recognized.[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-ACIE-2)[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-Steinfeld-3)

A _set_ of chemical reactions can be said to be "collectively autocatalytic" if a number of those reactions produce, as reaction products, catalysts for enough of the other reactions that the entire set of chemical reactions is self-sustaining given an input of energy and food molecules (see [autocatalytic set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalytic_set "Autocatalytic set")).

Acid-catalyzed hydrolysis of esters produces carboxylic acids that also catalyze the same reaction. Indeed, the observation of an accelerating hydrolysis of [gamma valerolactone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_valerolactone "Gamma valerolactone") to [gamma-hydroxyvaleric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-hydroxyvaleric_acid "Gamma-hydroxyvaleric acid") led to the introduction of the concept of autocatalysis in 1890.[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-Ostwald1890-4)

The oxidation of hydrocarbons by air or oxygen is the basis of [autoxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoxidation "Autoxidation"). Like many radical reactions, the rate vs time plot shows a [sigmoidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigmoid_function "Sigmoid function") behavior, characteristic of autocatalysis.[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-5) Many reactions of organic compounds with halogen involve autocatalytic radical [mechanisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_mechanism "Reaction mechanism"). For example the reaction of [acetophenone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetophenone "Acetophenone") with [bromine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromine "Bromine") to give [phenacyl bromide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenacyl_bromide "Phenacyl bromide").

[Oscillating reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_oscillator "Chemical oscillator") such as the [Belousov–Zhabotinsky reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belousov%E2%80%93Zhabotinsky_reaction "Belousov–Zhabotinsky reaction") are more complicated examples that involve autocatalysis.[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-ACIE-2) In such reactions the concentrations of some intermediates oscillate, as does the rate of formation of products. Other notable examples are the [Lotka–Volterra equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lotka%E2%80%93Volterra_equations "Lotka–Volterra equations") for the predator-prey model, and the [Brusselator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brusselator "Brusselator") model.

Autocatalysis applies also to reactions involving solids. [Crystal growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_growth "Crystal growth") provide dramatic examples of autocatalysis: the growth rate depends on the surface area of the growing crystal. The growth of metal films from solution using the technique of [electroless plating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroless_plating "Electroless plating") is autocatalytic. The rate of plating accelerates after some deposition has occurred, i.e., [nucleation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleation "Nucleation").[[6]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-6)

## Mathematical description

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Autocatalysis&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Mathematical description")]

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Sigmoid_curve_for_an_autocatalytical_reaction.jpg/330px-Sigmoid_curve_for_an_autocatalytical_reaction.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sigmoid_curve_for_an_autocatalytical_reaction.jpg)

Sigmoid variation of product concentration in autocatalytic reactions

Autocatalytic reactions are those in which at least one of the products is also a reactant. A simple autocatalytic reaction can be written[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-Steinfeld-3)

![Image 2: {\displaystyle A+B\rightleftharpoons 2B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/547407481a91eb7a0a8d16263298d419b34edca1)
with the rate equations (for an elementary reaction)

![Image 3: {\displaystyle {d \over dt}[A]=-k_{+}[A][B]+k_{-}[B]^{2}\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b56d2d10120edc5f81b31eb215a3c59121d2d89e)![Image 4: {\displaystyle {d \over dt}[B]=+k_{+}[A][B]-k_{-}[B]^{2}\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fe187f10f7df89b123d47bf155e81de07b7ec74).
This reaction is one in which a molecule of species A interacts with a molecule of species B. The A molecule is converted into a B molecule. The final product consists of the original B molecule plus the B molecule created in the reaction.

The key feature of these rate equations is that they are [nonlinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#Nonlinear_differential_equations "Nonlinear system"); the second term on the right varies as the square of the concentration of B. This feature can lead to multiple fixed points of the system, much like a [quadratic equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_equation "Quadratic equation") can have two roots. Multiple fixed points allow for multiple states of the system. A system existing in multiple [macroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroscopic "Macroscopic") states is more orderly (has lower entropy) than a system in a single state.

The concentrations of A and B vary in time according to

![Image 5: {\displaystyle [B]={\frac {[A]_{0}+[B]_{0}}{({\frac {[A]_{0}}{[B]_{0}}}-{\frac {k_{-}}{k_{+}}})e^{-k_{+}([A]_{0}+[B]_{0})t}+1+{\frac {k_{-}}{k_{+}}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/163e05fc7f14e6b67f11e2353aa16b917046ebab)
and

![Image 6: {\displaystyle [A]={\frac {([A]_{0}+[B]_{0})(({\frac {[A]_{0}}{[B]_{0}}}-{\frac {k_{-}}{k_{+}}})e^{-k_{+}([A]_{0}+[B]_{0})t}+{\frac {k_{-}}{k_{+}}})}{({\frac {[A]_{0}}{[B]_{0}}}-{\frac {k_{-}}{k_{+}}})e^{-k_{+}([A]_{0}+[B]_{0})t}+1+{\frac {k_{-}}{k_{+}}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26b73395451c13728d1389ee16852c49eab34d14).
For an irreversible reaction (i.e. ![Image 7: {\displaystyle k_{-}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f12788c7225a3ebd843db3a22138261b7e598f46))[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-Steinfeld-3)[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-Moore-7)

![Image 8: {\displaystyle [A]={\frac {[A]_{0}+[B]_{0}}{1+{\frac {[B]_{0}}{[A]_{0}}}e^{([A]_{0}+[B]_{0})kt}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/441bd0aee36b9fc56db162b05fd2e398be4c1d4d)
and

![Image 9: {\displaystyle [B]={\frac {[A]_{0}+[B]_{0}}{1+{\frac {[A]_{0}}{[B]_{0}}}e^{-([A]_{0}+[B]_{0})kt}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b5b30238c201994af2b92d366d4c3776b769a4f).
The graph for these equations is a [sigmoid curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigmoid_function "Sigmoid function") (specifically a [logistic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logistic_function "Logistic function")), which is typical for autocatalytic reactions: these chemical reactions proceed slowly at the start (the [induction period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Induction_period "Induction period")) because there is little catalyst present, the rate of reaction increases progressively as the reaction proceeds as the amount of catalyst increases and then it again slows down as the reactant concentration decreases. If the concentration of a reactant or product in an experiment follows a sigmoid curve, the reaction may be autocatalytic.

These kinetic equations apply for example to the acid-catalyzed hydrolysis of some [esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester "Ester") to [carboxylic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acid "Carboxylic acid") and [alcohols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_(chemistry) "Alcohol (chemistry)").[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-Moore-7) There must be at least some acid present initially to start the catalyzed mechanism; if not the reaction must start by an alternate uncatalyzed path which is usually slower. The above equations (which do not consider the alternate pathway) for the catalyzed mechanism would imply that the concentration of acid product remains zero forever.[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-Moore-7)

### Asymmetric autocatalysis

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Autocatalysis&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Asymmetric autocatalysis")]

Asymmetric autocatalysis occurs when the reaction product is [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiral "Chiral") and thus serves as a catalyst for its own production. Reactions of this type, such as the [Soai reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soai_reaction "Soai reaction"), have the property that they can amplify a very small [enantiomeric excess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enantiomeric_excess "Enantiomeric excess") into a large one.[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-8) In another example, [sodium chlorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chlorate "Sodium chlorate") crystallizes as an equilibrium mixture of left- and right-handed crystals. When seeded appropriated, saturated solutions of this salt (which is optically inactive), will produce batches of single enantiomeric crystals.[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-9)

### Possible role in origin of life

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Autocatalysis&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Possible role in origin of life")]

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/FormoseRxn.svg/330px-FormoseRxn.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:FormoseRxn.svg)

Autocatalytic cycle of formose reaction showing how glyceraldehyde can be both the catalyst and the product of one portion of this complex reaction type.

An early example of autocatalysis is the [formose reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formose_reaction "Formose reaction"), in which [formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde") and base produce sugars and related polyols. Characteristic of autocatalysis, this reaction rate is extremely slow initially but accelerates with time. This kind of reaction has often been cited as being relevant to the origin of life.[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-ACIE-2)

Autocatalysis is one explanation for [abiogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis").[[10]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-10)[[11]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-11)[[12]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-12) Illustrative is the reaction amino adenosine and pentafluorophenyl ester in the presence of amino adenosine triacid ester (AATE). This experiment demonstrated that autocatalysts could exhibit competition within a population of entities with heredity, which could be interpreted as a rudimentary form of [natural selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection "Natural selection"), and that certain environmental changes (such as irradiation) could alter the chemical structure of some of these self-replicating molecules (an analog for mutation) in such ways that could either boost or interfere with its ability to react, thus boosting or interfering with its ability to replicate and spread in the population.[[13]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocatalysis#cite_note-13)
