### TL;DR
Autocatalysis is a reaction pattern where a product catalyzes its own formation, producing nonlinear, often sigmoid kinetics and appearing in chemistry, crystallization, oscillating reactions, and origin-of-life models.

### Key Quote
“A chemical reaction is autocatalytic if one of the reaction products is also a catalyst for the same reaction.”

### Summary
- **Definition**
  - A reaction is **autocatalytic** when one of its products also acts as a catalyst for that same reaction.
  - A **collectively autocatalytic** set is a network of reactions where products catalyze enough other reactions that the whole set becomes self-sustaining with energy and food inputs.

- **Examples**
  - **Acid-catalyzed ester hydrolysis** can be autocatalytic because the carboxylic acid product catalyzes the same hydrolysis.
  - The accelerating hydrolysis of **gamma valerolactone** to **gamma-hydroxyvaleric acid** is cited as an early example tied to the concept’s introduction in **1890**.
  - **Autoxidation** of hydrocarbons shows sigmoid rate behavior typical of autocatalysis.
  - Some **organic halogenation reactions** proceed by autocatalytic radical mechanisms, including the example of **acetophenone + bromine → phenacyl bromide**.
  - **Oscillating reactions** such as the **Belousov–Zhabotinsky reaction** involve autocatalysis.
  - Related mathematical models include **Lotka–Volterra** predator-prey equations and the **Brusselator**.
  - Autocatalysis also applies to solids:
    - **Crystal growth** can be autocatalytic because growth rate increases with surface area.
    - **Electroless plating** becomes faster after nucleation and initial deposition.

- **Mathematical description**
  - A simple model is written as: **A + B ⇌ 2B**
  - The rate equations are nonlinear because the positive term depends on **[B]^2**.
  - Nonlinearity can create **multiple fixed points** and multiple macroscopic states.
  - For irreversible autocatalysis, concentration changes follow a **sigmoid/logistic curve**:
    - slow start due to little catalyst present,
    - faster mid-phase as catalyst accumulates,
    - slow finish as reactants are depleted.
  - The article notes that if an experimental concentration curve is sigmoid, the reaction may be autocatalytic.
  - For acid-catalyzed ester hydrolysis, some **initial acid** is needed to begin the catalyzed path; otherwise a slower uncatalyzed route must start the reaction.

- **Asymmetric autocatalysis**
  - Occurs when the product is **chiral** and catalyzes its own production.
  - The **Soai reaction** is highlighted as a key example.
  - These reactions can amplify a tiny **enantiomeric excess** into a large one.
  - **Sodium chlorate** crystallization is cited as another case where seeding can yield single-enantiomer crystals from an optically inactive solution.

- **Possible role in origin of life**
  - The **formose reaction** is presented as an early autocatalytic example: formaldehyde + base produce sugars/polyols, with the rate increasing over time.
  - Autocatalysis is discussed as one possible explanation for **abiogenesis**.
  - An experiment involving **amino adenosine**, **pentafluorophenyl ester**, and **AATE** is described as showing:
    - competition among self-replicating entities,
    - heredity-like behavior,
    - mutation-like effects from environmental changes such as irradiation.
  - This is framed as a rudimentary analog of **natural selection**.
