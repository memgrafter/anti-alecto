### TL;DR
This arXiv paper argues that generative models can sometimes **surpass the human experts they were trained on**—and demonstrates it by training a chess-playing autoregressive transformer on game transcripts, then showing it can occasionally outperform every player in the dataset.

### Key Quote
“In this work, we study the phenomenon of transcendence: when a generative model achieves capabilities that surpass the abilities of the experts generating its data.”

- **Main claim:**
  - Generative models trained by imitating human-produced data can sometimes exceed the performance of the humans in that data.
  - The authors call this phenomenon **“transcendence.”**

- **Core example / experiment:**
  - They train an **autoregressive transformer** on **chess game transcripts**.
  - Even though the model learns from expert/human game data, it can **sometimes achieve better performance than all players in the dataset**.

- **Why this is interesting:**
  - Standard intuition says a model trained to imitate a dataset should inherit the dataset’s limits.
  - The paper argues that this intuition is too weak: under some conditions, the learned generative model can become **better than its teachers**.

- **Theoretical contribution:**
  - The authors **prove that low-temperature sampling can enable transcendence**.
  - They then **experimentally assess** this theoretical claim.

- **Broader significance:**
  - The paper says it also discusses **other sources of transcendence**, suggesting the phenomenon may apply beyond chess and beyond this specific setup.
  - It is positioned as groundwork for future study of when generative models can exceed the capabilities encoded in their training data.
