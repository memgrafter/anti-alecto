### TL;DR
Dynomight reports a 16-month blinded self-experiment on L-theanine versus vitamin D placebo and concludes theanine showed no meaningful acute effect on stress, with his raw data looking similar or worse than placebo.

### Key Quote
“Blind trial or GTFO.”

### Summary
- **What this is:** A personal self-experiment post by Dynomight (Mar 2025) testing whether L-theanine actually reduces stress/anxiety in the short term.
- **Motivation:**
  - The internet widely praises theanine for anxiety, ADHD, memory, and mood.
  - Biological plausibility exists: theanine is structurally related to glutamate and may affect glutamate receptors; it can cross into the brain.
  - But the author found the academic evidence weak:
    - EFSA (2011) did **not** establish cause-and-effect for cognitive function, psychological stress, normal sleep, or menstrual discomfort.
    - Examine found **low-quality evidence** and near-zero effects across several uses.
    - A 2020 review suggested possible stress/anxiety benefit, but the author thinks it was too permissive.
    - A 2021 trial in 52 healthy older Japanese adults found mostly no cognitive benefit.
- **Experimental design:**
  - Compared:
    - **Capsule A:** 200 mg L-theanine
    - **Capsule B:** 25 mcg (1,000 IU) vitamin D
  - He chose vitamin D because it was:
    - same size/weight/texture as theanine capsule
    - similar in color
    - presumably not acutely anxiolytic
    - from a manufacturer he considered reasonably trustworthy
  - **Blinded randomization:** he put 2 theanine capsules and 1 vitamin D capsule into a cup, shook it, and took one without looking.
  - **Dosing rule:** took a capsule whenever he felt stressed/anxious; later switched to once or twice daily when data collection was too slow.
  - **Measurement:** recorded starting stress (1–5), waited about an hour, then recorded ending stress and predicted whether it had been theanine.
  - **Sample size:** 94 data points over about 16 months.
- **Results:**
  - Stress generally decreased over the hour, but it decreased **whether he took theanine or vitamin D**.
  - He was **poor at identifying** which capsule he had taken.
  - The author thinks the decline is likely due to **reversion to the mean** and/or placebo effects rather than a real theanine effect.
  - His explicit conclusion: theanine **doesn’t appear to do anything** in his experience.
- **Interpretation / possibilities considered:**
  - Theanine works but he got fake product
  - Theanine works but vitamin D works equally well
  - Theanine works but he was unlucky
  - Theanine works but he can’t reliably introspect his internal state
  - Theanine works for other people but not him
  - Theanine doesn’t work
  - He assigns the highest probability to **“doesn’t work”**
- **Comparison to other experiments:**
  - Mentions Niplav’s self-experiment, which was also negative (“hard pass”).
  - Cites a few blinded self-experiments:
    - caffeine positive
    - amphetamines positive
    - lithium no effect
    - LSD microdose no effect
    - ZMA inconclusive
    - sleep support no effect
  - Uses these as evidence that blinded N-of-1 tests are feasible and often humbling.
- **Statistical appendix:**
  - He warns the data are **not fully independent** because he could see outcomes trial-to-trial and adjusted expectations over time.
  - Still provides rough stats:
    - Stress went down significantly in both theanine and vitamin D trials.
    - The difference in **change in stress** between theanine and vitamin D was **not significant**.
    - Two significant findings appeared, but they were not supportive of theanine:
      - end stress slightly higher on theanine
      - prediction accuracy slightly different in the opposite direction than expected
  - His stance: if the effect isn’t visible in the raw data, he is not convinced by statistics alone.
- **Bottom line:**
  - The post argues that anecdotes and weak literature are not enough; if theanine works, proponents should demonstrate it in a **blinded trial**.
  - The author’s practical conclusion is a strong skepticism toward theanine as an acute anti-stress supplement.
