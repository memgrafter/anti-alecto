### TL;DR
Hacker News thread on the “shed vs skyscraper” metaphor for side projects and burnout in the AI era, where the top comment by `xamado` argues that hobby “shed” work restores joy and often helps work indirectly, but should not be treated as a career-advancement strategy.

### Key Quote
> “Tinker in your shed because it makes you happy, and brings joy and meaning to your life.”

### Summary
- **Top comment (verbatim):** “Tinker in your shed because it makes you happy, and brings joy and meaning to your life. You'll be more productive and, in my experience, you'll actually be more likely to learn something useful for work.”
- **Top commenter:** `xamado`
- **Thread topics:**
  - Burnout from AI-era software work and loss of enjoyment in coding
  - Whether hobby projects should stay separate from day-job engineering
  - “Shed” vs “skyscraper” as a metaphor for personal vs enterprise-scale work
  - Homelabs, overengineering, and the maintenance burden of personal infrastructure
  - Whether side projects can meaningfully feed back into professional work

- The thread starts from the idea that working on one’s own “shed” can help with burnout, especially when AI tools and agent-driven workflows make day jobs feel more detached from hands-on coding.
- Multiple commenters describe side projects as the place where they rediscovered:
  - coding for fun,
  - tinkering with hardware and reverse engineering,
  - using their hands in physical projects,
  - and the satisfaction of making something that exists outside corporate constraints.
- A strong countercurrent in the discussion is that hobby work and professional work are not interchangeable:
  - several commenters say the home “shed” should **not** be treated as unpaid career training,
  - some warn that letting work habits bleed into home projects turns the shed into another workplace,
  - others argue the reverse flow is often harmful because enterprise requirements, scale, and management constraints are very different.
- The “skyscraper” side of the metaphor is used to describe:
  - enterprise-grade systems,
  - large-scale maintenance,
  - corporate expectations,
  - and the fact that work often demands boring reliability over curiosity.
- The “shed” side is used to describe:
  - small, personal, curiosity-driven projects,
  - low-stakes experimentation,
  - learning by doing,
  - and the freedom to accept imperfection or throw things away.
- Several commenters emphasize practical boundaries:
  - don’t overengineer home projects,
  - prefer setups that are easy to declare, deploy, back up, and restore,
  - avoid turning homelabs into endless maintenance sinks,
  - and keep personal infrastructure separate from systems you depend on.
- There are also comments about:
  - leaving tech for more physical or real-world work,
  - accepting lower pay to escape “golden handcuffs,”
  - the difficulty of sustaining hobby programming with kids, partners, and demanding jobs,
  - and the emotional value of doing even 15–30 minutes of creative work regularly.
- A side discussion asks whether there should be a place to document and share shed projects, with mentions of:
  - Hackaday / Hackaday.io,
  - openhardware.directory,
  - ohwr.org,
  - and the idea of a “pay-it-forward” circle for inventions.
- The thread also includes skepticism about AI-generated code and AI-saturated professional life:
  - some commenters say personal projects are what keep them sane,
  - others say they now need to perform enthusiasm for AI initiatives at work despite misgivings.
- Overall consensus: the shed metaphor resonates because personal projects give many people joy, agency, and a sense of craftsmanship, but the exact value of cross-pollinating between hobby and work depends heavily on scale, constraints, and personality.
