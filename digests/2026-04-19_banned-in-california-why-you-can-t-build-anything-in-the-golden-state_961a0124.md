### TL;DR
A visual argument that California’s permitting and environmental rules have made it effectively impossible to start many modern industrial facilities today, so the state relies on grandfathered legacy plants to build cars, chips, batteries, ships, and other advanced products.

### Key Quote
“Every component in your pocket required an industrial process that's nearly impossible to permit in California today.”

### Summary
- The page is a **visual guide** ranking industrial processes in California by feasibility:
  - **Impossible**
  - **Extremely difficult**
  - **Restricted / heavily regulated**
- Main thesis: **California can still host old industrial sites, but new greenfield industrial facilities are often treated as practically impossible to permit** due to air quality, wastewater, hazardous chemicals, emissions, and other regulations.
- It argues that many products people use every day depend on processes that California would not realistically allow to be newly built today, including:
  - semiconductor fabrication
  - chemical etching
  - metal anodizing
  - glass tempering
  - electroplating
  - battery cell manufacturing
  - foundry work
  - paint shops
- The page uses examples from **consumer electronics**, **EV manufacturing**, and **naval shipbuilding** to make the case:
  - A smartphone’s components rely on chip fabs, PCB etching, glass processing, and plating.
  - EV production needs paint shops, battery cells, casting, motor components, glass, copper, and semiconductor parts.
  - Naval shipbuilding requires steel rolling, welding, superalloy casting, radar and electronics fabrication, plating, coatings, and heavy metal fabrication.
- Specific claims and examples include:
  - **No new semiconductor fabs** have been built in California in over a decade.
  - **Tesla’s Gigafactory** went to **Reno, Nevada** because California permitting made battery cell manufacturing effectively impossible.
  - **Tesla’s Cybertruck factory** went to **Austin, Texas**.
  - Tesla’s **Fremont** factory is described as **grandfathered in**, since it was originally the **NUMMI** plant and dates to **1962**.
  - **General Dynamics NASSCO** in **San Diego** is presented as one of the only West Coast yards able to build large naval vessels, and as a grandfathered facility that could not easily be replaced if closed.
- The industrial categories called out include:
  - **Semiconductor fabrication (7nm/5nm)**
  - **Aluminum anodizing & CNC machining**
  - **Lithium-ion cell manufacturing**
  - **PCB etching & component soldering**
  - **Optical lens coating & sensor fab**
  - **Glass tempering & chemical strengthening**
  - **RF component fab & gold plating**
  - **Automotive paint shops**
  - **Metal stamping, forging & die casting**
  - **SiC/GaN power semiconductor fab**
  - **Copper winding & magnet production**
  - **Float glass manufacturing**
  - **Iron casting & brake manufacturing**
  - **Rubber vulcanization**
  - For shipbuilding: **steel plate rolling, shipyard welding, superalloy casting, antenna manufacturing, missile canister fabrication, gun barrel forging, propeller casting, power generation equipment, military-grade electronics, anti-fouling coatings**
- The page’s closing message says these facilities survive because they were built before current rules, and if they closed they could not reopen under the same permits.
- It ends with a political/organizational pitch:
  - “We’re building a coalition for everyone who thinks the Golden State should make things again.”
