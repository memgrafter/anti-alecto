### TL;DR
Video Game History Foundation argues that *Cookie’s Bustle* is an orphaned 1999 Japanese game and that Brandon White/Graceware had no meaningful proof of ownership, leading Ukie to suspend takedowns that had been used to suppress preservation and fair-use material.

### Key Quote
“we do not believe there is any proof that Brandon White actually owns Cookie’s Bustle.”

### Summary
- **What *Cookie’s Bustle* is**
  - A 1999 computer game by Japanese developer/publisher RODIK, Inc.
  - The article highlights its bizarre cult status with the premise: you play a **5-year-old girl from New Jersey** who enters an international sports competition that becomes tangled in **civil war and intergalactic intrigue**.
  - VGHF received a rare physical copy from multimedia researcher **Misty De Méo** and documented the packaging and physical materials in its digital archive.
  - Because the game is out of print and not shareable for controlled access, VGHF also provides a gameplay video recorded by community member **sebmal**.

- **The dispute**
  - Since 2021, **Brandon White** and/or **Graceware, SL** has claimed copyright and filed trademark applications related to *Cookie’s Bustle*.
  - VGHF says it found **no legally meaningful evidence** that White owns enforceable rights in the game.
  - The group describes Graceware’s behavior as an aggressive **copyright-trolling / rights-squatting campaign**.

- **What Graceware allegedly did**
  - Sent repeated DMCA takedowns, via **Ukie** and **Web Capio** (later doing business as **Obviously**), against:
    - playthroughs and livestreams
    - screenshots and clips
    - fanart
    - an English-subtitle add-on
    - Discord messages discussing the game
  - VGHF itself received takedowns for archive materials, including a page that merely said it owned a copy and listed the game files as **“Not Available”**.
  - The article says these takedowns were often obviously fair use or otherwise inappropriate.

- **Why VGHF says White’s claims are weak**
  - **INTEROCO “copyright registrations”**:
    - White relied on deposits made through INTEROCO, described as a private German “full-automated electronic depository,” not a government copyright office.
    - VGHF argues these deposits do **not** establish copyright ownership for a work already published in 1999.
    - VGHF tested INTEROCO itself and found confusing, unreliable processes, including an alleged third-party contact in Dubai claiming $12,000 per item.
  - **Trademark applications**:
    - Graceware filed U.S. “intent to use” trademark applications for **Cookie’s Bustle** in December 2022.
    - These were still pending and had required four extensions as of the article.
    - VGHF emphasizes that pending trademark applications do **not** prove copyright ownership.
    - The original RODIK trademark for the name had expired in **2001**.

- **How the takedown system was used**
  - VGHF says Graceware relied on **cheap, low-oversight legal services** and automated systems.
  - Ukie’s IP service, via **Mumith Ali / Web Capio**, reportedly sent bulk takedowns with limited human review.
  - Graceware’s notices were often directed at **service providers** like **Vercel**, not just the actual hosts of the content, because platforms are more likely to comply quickly.
  - VGHF notes this is a weakness of the DMCA process: platforms often remove material first to avoid liability.

- **What changed**
  - VGHF raised the issue with **Ukie CEO Nick Poole** and then with **Mumith Ali**.
  - After being asked to clarify the ownership claim, Graceware did **not** provide sufficient proof.
  - According to the post, **Ukie suspended DMCA takedown services for Graceware**.
  - VGHF frames this as a victory against a long-running abuse campaign, but not a final legal resolution of ownership.

- **What the article says this means**
  - The real owner of *Cookie’s Bustle* remains **unknown**, so it is likely still an **orphan work**.
  - The game is **not public domain**.
  - In practice, the article says fair uses such as gameplay clips, streams, commentary, documentation, and fan works should be less vulnerable to these particular takedowns for now.
  - However, VGHF warns Graceware could still try other routes, and the underlying issues with INTEROCO and automated takedowns remain.

- **Practical advice offered**
  - If contacted by Graceware, ask for proof of:
    - copyright ownership, or
    - an exclusive license for the original 1999 game
  - If content is removed unfairly, file a **DMCA counterclaim** on the relevant platform.
  - Potentially consider **17 U.S.C. § 512(f)** if false takedowns cause harm.

- **Broader argument**
  - VGHF ties this case to a larger preservation problem: many pre-1995 games/software may be orphan works.
  - Because video games were often treated as disposable, they are especially vulnerable to bad documentation, disinformation, and copyright abuse.
  - The article positions the fight over *Cookie’s Bustle* as part of a broader defense of historians’, archivists’, and creators’ rights to document and discuss out-of-print games.
