### TL;DR
Wikipedia’s “Path dependence” article explains how early events or choices can lock in later outcomes across economics, politics, technology, and organizations, with classic examples like VHS vs. Betamax, QWERTY, and railway gauge standards.

### Key Quote
“the future development of an economic system is affected by the path it has traced out in the past”

### Summary
- **Core idea:** path dependence means past events or decisions constrain or shape later events or decisions.
- It is used in two main ways:
  - the broad “history matters” claim
  - the stronger claim that early events can have **disproportionate, irreversible, or locking-in effects** through mechanisms like:
    - increasing returns
    - positive feedback
    - lock-in
    - hysteresis
    - switching costs/barriers
- The concept applies to:
  - institutions
  - technical standards
  - economic development
  - social development
  - organizational behavior

#### Commercial / technology examples
- **VHS vs. Betamax**
  - VHS became dominant through mechanisms independent of pure product quality:
    - network effects in rental stores
    - manufacturer bandwagoning
    - Sony’s refusal to license Betamax for pornography production
  - The article also notes a counterview: VHS may simply have better matched market demand, especially longer recording time.
- **QWERTY keyboard**
  - Often cited as an example of persistence of a possibly less efficient standard.
  - The article notes debate over whether Dvorak or other layouts are actually better, so the example is contested.
- **Railway track gauge**
  - Standard gauge (4 ft 8+1⁄2 in / 143.5 cm) spread from Stephenson’s early choices on the Liverpool and Manchester Railway.
  - Wider gauges may offer performance advantages, but the historical choice spread globally through copying and standardization.
  - The article frames this as a case where an early, somewhat arbitrary choice shaped long-run technology adoption.

#### Economics
- Path dependence was originally developed by economists to explain:
  - technology adoption
  - industry evolution
- Key economic claims:
  - small initial differences can amplify into large later outcomes
  - outcomes may not converge to a single equilibrium
  - multiple equilibria or absorbing states can result
  - noise and accidental events can matter a lot
- Examples mentioned:
  - **economic development and standards**: first-to-market standards can become entrenched
  - **agglomeration**: businesses cluster geographically, attracting workers and more firms
  - **exchange-rate shocks and factory closures**: reopening can become too costly even after conditions improve
  - **adaptive expectations**: past inflation shapes expected future inflation
  - **unemployment hysteresis**: temporary unemployment can create persistent structural unemployment

#### Debates and theory
- **Liebowitz and Margolis** distinguish types of path dependence and argue only a stronger “third-degree” version seriously challenges neoclassical economics; they claim true inefficient lock-in is rare.
- **Vergne and Durand** respond by specifying conditions under which path dependence can be tested empirically.
- Technically, a path-dependent stochastic process is described as having an asymptotic distribution that evolves as a function of its own history; this is linked to **non-ergodic** processes.
- **Edith Penrose** is cited for showing how firm growth depends on managerial experience and firm history.

#### Conditions that can produce path dependence
- Durability of capital equipment
- Technical interrelatedness
- Increasing returns
- Dynamic increasing returns to adoption

#### Social sciences / institutions
- In political science and sociology, the concept is used to explain institutional persistence and change.
- Two main patterns are described:
  - **Critical junctures**
    - antecedent conditions allow contingent choices
    - those choices set a trajectory that becomes hard to reverse
    - associated with Ruth and David Collier
  - **Reactive sequences**
    - one event triggers a tight chain of later events
- Examples:
  - welfare state development
  - labor incorporation in Latin America
  - cross-country differences in development
- **Kathleen Thelen** cautions against treating politics like QWERTY-style lock-in; she argues the processes are both more contingent and less deterministic than that analogy suggests.
- The article cites work claiming legal traditions can still affect court rulings long after formal legal unification.

#### Organizations and individuals
- **Paul Pierson** is noted for formalizing path dependence in political science.
- **Herman Schwartz** criticizes applying economic-style lock-in too broadly in politics.
- A related concept in sociology/organizational theory is **imprinting**: early conditions leave lasting marks on organizations.
- Experiments have observed path dependence in emergent strategy among individuals and groups.

#### Other examples
- **Typological vestiges**: customs that persist after their original rationale disappears, such as U.S. punctuation conventions.
- **Evolution**: historical mutations can have long-lasting effects, even if no longer adaptive.
- **Software and computing**: legacy compatibility requirements create lock-in and design compromises.
- **Commercial fisheries**: harvest rates and conservation outcomes can be path dependent.
- **Physics/math**: non-holonomic systems depend on the path taken.
