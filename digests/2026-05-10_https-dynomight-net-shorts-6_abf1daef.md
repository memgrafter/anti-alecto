- **Pointing machines / sculpture**
  - The author starts with Michelangelo and the question of how marble sculptures are made without mistakes.
  - They describe the **pointing machine**: a tool used to copy a clay sculpture into stone by transferring measurements from anchor points.
  - Then they correct themselves: Michelangelo died in **1564**, long before the pointing machine was invented.
  - The more accurate historical precursor is the **“compass method”**, used since ancient times, including by Romans copying Greek sculptures.

- **Population pyramids**
  - The author calls population pyramids one of the greatest data visualizations ever invented.
  - They note the chart is basically a histogram turned sideways, but it compresses both **past demographic history** and **future population implications** into one image.
  - They attribute the invention to **Francis Amasa Walker** and point to his use of the form in the **1874 US atlas**.
  - The atlas apparently contains other visualizations the author finds beautiful, including:
    - **church attendance**
    - **debt and public expenditures**

- **British Post Office scandal**
  - The post explains the scandal in brief:
    - In **1999**, Fujitsu software gave the British Post Office bad accounting data.
    - The system falsely indicated that thousands of subpostmasters were stealing.
    - Based on this, the Post Office prosecuted and convicted nearly **1,000 people**; **236 went to prison**.
    - Many more lost jobs or paid alleged “shortfalls” out of pocket.
  - The author’s reaction is that the case is horrifying but also puzzling:
    - It seems driven by **arrogance, stupidity, and negligence**, not deliberate malice.
    - They expected some institutional check would stop such widespread punishment based on bad evidence, but it didn’t.
  - They speculate about possible explanations:
    - The Post Office’s unusual power to bring prosecutions itself
    - Or the broader fact that prosecutors have wide latitude and can often convict innocents when armed with vaguely incriminating evidence
  - They suggest many similar injustices may happen in smaller, less visible cases that never become major news.

- **Type species**

  - They conclude the original jargon is so convoluted that it’s hard to outdo it.
  - They add a concrete curiosity: the **Sri Lankan elephant** is the type species for the **Asian elephant**.

- **Hormones / pregnant mares’ urine**

  - They note that **Premarin** literally comes from **PREgnant MARe’s urINe**.
  - They cite **The History of Estrogen Therapy** and point out that this was happening as early as **1941**.
  - Even earlier, urine from pregnant humans was used for similar purposes.
  - They jokingly note the unfortunate naming alternative that was not used: **“Prehumin.”**
