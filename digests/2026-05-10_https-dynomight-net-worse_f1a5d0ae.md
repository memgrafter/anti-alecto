### TL;DR
Dynomight argues that “make product worse, get money” is too shallow a theory for bad products like dating apps, because many goods are constrained by consumer willingness to pay, information asymmetries, taste, or market power—not just seller greed.

### Key Quote
“In the conflict between these desires that all interesting theories live.”

### Summary
- The piece starts from a common critique of dating apps: if apps successfully match people into long-term relationships, users would leave, so the apps supposedly have an incentive to keep people on the platform instead of helping them.
- The author says this kind of theory is not very useful because it can be applied almost anywhere:
  - pizza restaurants could use cheaper ingredients,
  - airlines could cram more seats,
  - automakers could make unsafe cars,
  - bloggers could do sloppy research,
  - manufacturers could make products that break sooner.
- These all reduce to two broad “bad product” theories:
  - **Make product worse, get money**
  - **Raise price, get money**
- The problem is that these theories ignore the counterforce of competition and consumer preferences:
  - If one seller makes a worse product or charges too much, another seller can undercut them.
  - Markets tend toward an equilibrium where sellers cannot simply maximize profit by making everything awful.
- The article then lays out four main reasons stuff is bad:
  - **People are cheap**
    - Consumers often prefer a worse-but-cheaper product.
    - Example: airlines might not remove seats because passengers may not actually pay more for extra legroom.
    - This explains cramped flights, soggy Subway sandwiches, buggy video games, and disposable furniture/clothes.
  - **Information asymmetries**
    - Consumers often cannot tell quality from fraud.
    - This helps explain bad longevity content, dubious supplements, unsafe food contaminants, poor insulation in rentals, and cars that are only safe in tested dimensions.
    - The author’s point: “When consumers can’t tell good from evil, evil triumphs.”
  - **People have bad taste**
    - Sometimes people genuinely don’t value what the author values.
    - Examples given include bad restaurants in some cities, North American tea, and travel pants.
  - **Pricing power**
    - Some firms have moats: regulation, tech barriers, IP, brand, resource control, or network effects.
    - In these cases, firms can degrade quality or raise prices because competitors can’t easily discipline them.
- The conclusion uses sporting-event food as an example of a place where high prices seem to persist because venues can extract money from captive, segmented audiences.
- The author returns to dating apps:
  - Yes, they may have incentives to keep users paying.
  - But the real question is not whether that incentive exists; it’s **why they can get away with it**.
  - The likely answers are either that better competition is impossible or that customers don’t actually want the better alternative enough to support it.
