### TL;DR
Dynomight argues that a hypothetical IQ-300, 10,000×-faster AI would be dramatically better at thinking but still constrained by physics, data, experiments, hardware, and human institutions—so it might rapidly improve math, programming, and some persuasion, but would not automatically solve weather, cancer, chess, or geopolitics.

### Key Quote
> “A super-intelligence wouldn’t be a god.”

### Summary
- **Core thesis:** raw intelligence alone does not instantly solve every problem, because many important tasks depend on:
  - **new fundamental knowledge** from experiments,
  - **situational knowledge** gathered from the world,
  - **moving molecules around** via physical infrastructure,
  - **feedback loops** between research, hardware, factories, and energy,
  - and **chaos/complexity** that limits prediction even with better reasoning.
- The post uses a thought experiment: imagine an AI with the combined talents of “Von Neumann, Archimedes, Ramanujan, and Mozart,” then multiplied several times again, with **IQ 300** and **10,000× normal speed**, but **no extra resources** beyond what humans already have.

- **Math / programming / philosophy**
  - Likely the easiest wins.
  - The author thinks these domains are “plausibly” solvable quickly because progress mostly requires thinking.
  - This is where superhuman intelligence should matter most.

- **Weather prediction**
  - Only modest gains at first.
  - Better models still depend on **supercomputers**, **satellites**, **radar**, and accurate initial conditions.
  - Weather is chaotic, so prediction horizons are limited by data and compute, not just intelligence.
  - **Best guess:** a bit better, not magical.

- **2024 U.S. election prediction / geopolitics**
  - Probably not fully knowable from available polling, economic data, and history.
  - The author argues the decisive information was buried in millions of people’s private intentions.
  - Better forecasts and betting-market profits? Likely yes.
  - Perfect certainty? No.
  - **Best guess:** no psychic-level certainty.

- **Chess**
  - Unclear whether the AI would beat top engines.
  - Current chess engines are described as extremely strong: top engine Elo **3625**.
  - The author argues modern engines rely on **deep tree search** over huge numbers of positions (on the order of **100,000,000 positions/second**), which “intelligence” alone may not replace.
  - A 10,000× faster thinker could search more than a human, but probably not like a chess engine.
  - **Best guess:** unsure; maybe loses in complex endgames.

- **Creativity / art**
  - The AI would likely be outstanding at novels, music, jokes, etc.
  - But the author suggests normal-human art may remain valuable because:
    - taste can be socially constructed (via a Bourdieu-style view),
    - and art can matter because it reflects **human experience**, not just technical excellence.
  - The Being may not know “what it’s like to walk in your shoes.”
  - **Best guess:** great art, but human art still matters.

- **Physics**
  - Maybe a super-intelligence could infer a unifying pattern from current experiments.
  - Or maybe not: observations may be consistent with too many possible theories.
  - The author notes that quantum physics was not straightforwardly predicted; it had to be invented after strange observations.
  - If the theory isn’t already latent in the data, the AI would mainly help design **new experiments**.
  - **Best guess:** probably not immediate breakthroughs.

- **Cancer / biology**
  - The author says biologists they asked overwhelmingly answered **“no”** to “would superintelligence cure cancer?”
  - Reason: biology is limited not mainly by intelligence, but by **experimental knowledge** and the need for new tools.
  - Modern biology is described as cumulative and tool-driven:
    - tools to build tools,
    - techniques like **TALENs, molecular beacons, phage display, ChIP-seq, bioluminescence imaging, prime editing**.
  - The AI might accelerate the research loop, but not bypass it.
  - **Best guess:** no immediate cure.

- **Persuasion / diplomacy**
  - The AI could be extremely persuasive if it had perfect rhetoric and emotional calibration.
  - But the author doubts there are many people whose minds can be changed on deeply polarized issues like **Trump, abortion, Israel, or Ukraine** merely by words.
  - Most people have strong defensive attitudes.
  - **Best guess:** no idea; could be powerful, but there are hard limits.

- **Repeated themes / model of the future**
  - Some tasks require **fundamental knowledge**.
  - Some require **situational knowledge**.
  - Many require **physical infrastructure**.
  - Many are constrained by **feedback loops** and **complexity**.
  - The likely path is not instant godhood, but a **gradual acceleration loop**:
    - better AI helps research,
    - research helps robotics and hardware,
    - better hardware helps AI,
    - and the cycle speeds up over time.
  - The author suggests the first-order effect may be best thought of as a **multiplier on economic growth**, not instant total replacement of humans.

- **Looking ahead**
  - The author expects that if superintelligent AI arrives soon, people may later look back and feel they missed obvious implications.
  - One especially important implication mentioned: **computer security will matter a lot**, and critical systems should stay simple and isolated from the internet.
  - But the post’s main caution is that even superintelligence still has to operate in the real world.
