### TL;DR
Barry R. Chiswick reviews Joel Mokyr’s *A Culture of Growth*, arguing that the Industrial Revolution and sustained Western growth were driven by a long-run cultural shift toward scientific, useful knowledge, aided by Europe’s competitive, decentralized institutions and contrasted with China’s different incentives.

### Key Quote
> “It seems wrong to dub the Chinese experience a ‘failure.’ What is exceptional, indeed unique, is what happened in eighteenth-century Europe.”

### Summary
- **Content type:** Book review from *Finance & Development* (December 2017, Vol. 54, No. 1) of Joel Mokyr’s *A Culture of Growth: The Origins of the Modern Economy* (Princeton University Press, 2017).
- **Reviewer:** Barry R. Chiswick, Professor of Economics and International Affairs at George Washington University.
- **Central thesis of the book:**
  - Mokyr places **culture** at the center of Europe’s economic transformation.
  - He argues the **Enlightenment** and the **Industrial Revolution** were not exogenous shocks, but the result of a centuries-long shift in beliefs about science, nature, and human control over the world.
  - This “culture of growth” emerged in Western Europe between roughly **1500 and 1700** and helped produce sustained economic growth.

- **Key intellectual drivers Mokyr emphasizes:**
  - **Francis Bacon**: promoted the idea that science should generate “new discoveries and resources” for human life.
  - **Isaac Newton**: showed that nature operates according to discoverable mathematical regularities.
  - Their influence spread through an **open marketplace of ideas**, where ideas could be “distributed and shared, and hence challenged, corrected and supplemented.”

- **Mechanisms that helped the cultural change spread:**
  - Improved **navigation and shipbuilding** expanded contact with new goods and ideas.
  - The **printing press** lowered communication costs and increased the value of literacy.
  - Europe’s **political fragmentation**—no single central authority—encouraged competition.
  - **Individual freedom**, **property rights**, and competition in markets for both goods and ideas supported innovation.
  - These factors helped convert Enlightenment ideas into the **Industrial Revolution** and then into **sustained economic growth**.

- **China as the counterexample:**
  - Mokyr uses China to show why growth did not happen everywhere, even where literacy and technology were high.
  - He points to:
    - reverence for **classical Chinese literature**
    - a **centralized government** that reduced interregional competition
    - selection of administrators based on literary knowledge rather than **science and technology**
    - weaker competitive pressures than in Europe
  - The result was a culture that rewarded different kinds of achievement, but not the ones that produced an industrial revolution.
  - Mokyr’s conclusion, as quoted in the review, is that the real anomaly is **Europe’s eighteenth-century transformation**, not a Chinese “failure.”

- **Assessment of the book by Chiswick:**
  - He praises Mokyr for explaining a complex topic with **big-picture clarity** and rich detail.
  - He says the book is accessible to both **general readers** and **economic history specialists**.
  - He recommends it as a **must-read** for understanding:
    - how Western society developed
    - what that history implies for the spread of technology in the global economy
