### TL;DR
This paper argues that “Agentic AI” and “Multiagentic AI” are mostly buzzwords for long-established ideas in AI—intelligent agents and multi-agent systems—and urges researchers to use standard terminology and decades of prior work instead of reinventing the wheel.

### Key Quote
“AI Agentic is often used as a buzzword for what are essentially AI agents, and AI Multiagentic for what are multi-agent systems.”

### Summary
- **Topic and thesis**
  - The article is a critical analysis of the recent popularity of the terms **Agentic AI** and **Multiagentic AI** in generative AI discourse.
  - The author argues these terms often confuse or relabel established concepts rather than introduce genuinely new ones.
  - Core claim: much of what is now marketed as “agentic” AI maps onto **intelligent agents**, and “multiagentic” AI maps onto **multi-agent systems**.

- **Conceptual background**
  - The paper traces the word **“agentic”** to social science origins, especially **Bandura (1986)**.
  - It also references philosophical work on **intentionality**, including **Dennett (1971)**.
  - From there, it connects to classic AI literature on **intelligent agents** and **multi-agent systems**, citing foundational work by **Wooldridge, Jennings, and others**.

- **AI agent foundations reviewed**
  - The article summarizes classic agent architectures:
    - **Simple reactive agents**
    - **Belief-Desire-Intention (BDI) models**
  - It highlights the defining properties of agency in AI:
    - **Autonomy**
    - **Reactivity**
    - **Proactivity**
    - **Social capability**

- **Relation to recent LLM-based systems**
  - The paper discusses newer developments in **large language models (LLMs)** and LLM-based agent platforms.
  - It notes the emergence of:
    - **LLM-powered AI agents**
    - **Open-source multi-agent orchestration frameworks**
  - The argument is not that these are unimportant, but that they should be understood in continuity with prior agent and multi-agent research rather than as wholly new conceptual territory.

- **Main critique**
  - The author sees “Agentic AI” and “Multiagentic AI” as buzzwords that can obscure the rich prior literature.
  - This includes decades of work on:
    - **Standards for multi-agent system platforms**
    - **Communication languages**
    - **Coordination and cooperation algorithms**
    - **Agreement technologies**, such as:
      - automated negotiation
      - argumentation
      - virtual organisations
      - trust
      - reputation

- **Conclusion / recommendation**
  - The paper advocates for **scientific and technological rigour**.
  - It recommends using established terminology and incorporating the accumulated knowledge of autonomous agents and multi-agent systems into modern LLM-based agent development.
  - The phrase “so as not to end up reinventing the wheel” captures the paper’s central warning.
